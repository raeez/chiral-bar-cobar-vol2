\section{$W$-type Poisson vertex algebras and higher-spin celestial data}
\label{sec:cbt-w-type}

For $W$-type PVAs in the sense of Khan--Zeng~\cite{KZ25}, the abstract obstruction theory specializes to concrete higher-spin invariants.

\subsection{The abstract $W$-type principle}
\label{sec:cbt-abstract-w-type}

By Theorem~\ref{thm:cbt-lowest-nonlinearity}, the first nonlinear celestial invariant associated to a PVA is controlled only by the lowest nonlinear homogeneous part of the generator $\lambda$-brackets. This becomes especially concrete for $W$-type PVAs, where the first nonlinear piece is often quadratic.

\begin{corollary}[first higher-spin correction for quadratic $W$-type PVAs]
\label{cor:cbt-w-type-quadratic}
\ClaimStatusConditional
Let $V$ be a freely generated $W$-type PVA, and assume:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2em]
\item the boundary/celestial tree-level transfer exists and respects the composite filtration;
\item the first nonlinear homogeneous term in the generator $\lambda$-brackets is quadratic.
\end{enumerate}
Then the first nonlinear obstruction class $\operatorname{Ob}_1$ of the associated boundary/celestial $\Ainf$-algebra depends only on the linearized Lie conformal structure and the quadratic part of the $\lambda$-brackets. No cubic or higher homogeneous term can affect $\operatorname{Ob}_1$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
This is the case $q=2$ of Theorem~\ref{thm:cbt-lowest-nonlinearity}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The \texorpdfstring{$W_3$}{W3} example}
\label{sec:cbt-w3-example}

Khan and Zeng describe the classical $W_3$ PVA as generated by a spin-$2$ field $T$ and a spin-$3$ field $W$ with
\[
\{T_\lambda T\}=\partial T+2T\lambda+\frac{c}{12}\lambda^3,
\]
\[
\{T_\lambda W\}=\partial W+3W\lambda,
\]
and
\[
\{W_\lambda W\}
=
\frac{c}{360}\lambda^5
+\frac13 T\lambda^3
+\frac12(\partial T)\lambda^2
+\left(\frac{3}{10}\partial^2T+\frac{16}{22+5c}T^2\right)\lambda
+\frac1{15}\partial^3T
+\frac{16}{22+5c}T\,\partial T.
\]
The lowest nonlinear part is quadratic in $T$.

\begin{corollary}[first higher-spin obstruction for the classical \texorpdfstring{$W_3$}{W3} PVA]
\label{cor:cbt-w3-obstruction}
\ClaimStatusConditional
Assuming the tree-level filtered boundary transfer exists for the $W_3$ PVA and respects composite degree, the first nonlinear celestial/boundary obstruction class is controlled entirely by the linear Virasoro-$W$ sector together with the quadratic terms
\[
\frac{16}{22+5c}T^2\lambda
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\frac{16}{22+5c}T\,\partial T
\]
in $\{W_\lambda W\}$. No higher nonlinear correction can influence the first obstruction.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The $W_3$ bracket displayed above has first nonlinear homogeneous degree $2$. Apply Corollary~\ref{cor:cbt-w-type-quadratic}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:cbt-universal-first-correction}
This explains the existence of a \emph{universal first higher-spin celestial correction}: once the tree-level filtered transfer exists and the linear conformal algebra together with the first quadratic nonlinear term are fixed, the first obstruction is fixed.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[beyond $W_3$]
\label{rem:cbt-beyond-w3}
Khan--Zeng emphasize that their construction extends to other $W_n$ and $W_\infty$ algebras. Conditional on the tree-level filtered transfer, the abstract theorem above therefore predicts a hierarchy of first nonlinear higher-spin celestial invariants before explicit transfer formulas are worked out.
\end{remark}

\section{Synthesis: the platonic picture}
\label{sec:cbt-synthesis}

The constructions above reveal a single mathematical object seen under three projections.

\subsection*{I. The wedge projection}
Quotient by composites. This produces the wedge $\Ainf$-algebra $\mathbb{W}(C)$, which is the intrinsic single-particle shadow of the full boundary/celestial algebra.

\subsection*{II. The bar-filtered projection}
Keep composites, but organize them by filtration. This produces the obstruction tower
\[
\operatorname{Ob}_1,\operatorname{Ob}_2,\operatorname{Ob}_3,\dots
\]
and the composite spectral sequence. These classes are the nonlinear curvature of the celestial algebra relative to its wedge sector.

\subsection*{III. The highest-weight projection}
Restrict to exactly solvable weight sectors. This is where the transfer becomes explicit: simplex integrals, simplex summation laws, double factorials, exact $\pi_n$ coefficients, and finally the one-dimensional Airy--Witt normal form.

\begin{quote}
The boundary/celestial algebra is a filtered cotangent deformation. The wedge quotient is its linear shadow. The obstruction classes are its nonlinear curvature. The highest-weight ladders are its integrable geodesics.
\end{quote}

\section{Concrete open problems that remain after the present chapter}
\label{sec:cbt-open-problems}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item \textbf{Even-mode closure for the Poisson ladder.}
The Airy--Witt theorem computes the odd positive modes. One should identify the missing even modes inside the full transferred $\Linf$ algebra on $T^*[1]\operatorname{Ham}(\mathbb{C}^2)$.

\item \textbf{Central extensions on the ladder.}
Zeng explains that the higher Kac--Moody algebra admits central extensions. It is natural to transfer the cocycle to the highest-weight ladder and compute the resulting central higher brackets exactly.

\item \textbf{Explicit $W_3$ transfer.}
The abstract first-obstruction theorem is already in place. The next hard computation is to construct an explicit contraction and compute $\operatorname{Ob}_1$ and the first ladder sector for the $W_3$ PVA.

\item \textbf{Homotopy invariance of the higher ladder sectors.}
The all-arity formulas were proved for the canonical transferred model. Which of these define genuine gauge-invariant higher obstructions, and which are artifacts of the preferred deformation retract?

\item \textbf{Quantum corrections.}
The present chapter is tree-level. The next layer is to combine these exact formulas with loop corrections, especially in the Poisson-vertex setting where deformation quantization of the boundary algebra is a central problem.
\end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
