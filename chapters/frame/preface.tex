\section*{I.\quad Two directions}

\noindent
Volume~I built a single organising structure from a single
observation: every algebraic structure is a Maurer--Cartan element
in a convolution dg Lie algebra, and passing from algebras to
operads to modular operads replaces sequences by trees by stable
graphs.  The universal MC element
$\Theta_\cA \in \operatorname{MC}(\gAmod)$
encodes the full genus tower of a chiral algebra on a curve~$X$,
and every theorem of Volume~I is a projection of~$\Theta_\cA$.

The modular convolution uses one direction: the holomorphic
direction along~$X$.  A three-dimensional theory on
$\C \times \R$ introduces a second direction---topological,
along~$\R$---and forces the convolution to split into two
independent channels.  This is the Swiss-cheese phenomenon.

Two operators $a(z_1, t_1)$ and $b(z_2, t_2)$ in $\C
\times \R$ interact in two independent directions.

In the \emph{holomorphic direction}, the collision
$z_1 \to z_2$ produces the OPE residue $a_{(n)}b$.
Let $\cA$ be a chiral algebra on a smooth curve.
The \emph{bar complex} $\barBch(\cA) = (T^c(s^{-1}\bar\cA), D_\cA)$
is the cofree conilpotent coalgebra on the desuspended augmentation
ideal, with differential $D_\cA$ encoding the OPE data of~$\cA$.
Here $T^c(V) = \bigoplus_{n \ge 0}(V^{\otimes n})_{\Sigma_n}$ is the cofree conilpotent coalgebra with deconcatenation coproduct, $s^{-1}$ is the desuspension ($|s^{-1}a| = |a| - 1$), and $\bar\cA = \ker(\varepsilon\colon \cA \to \omega_X)$ is the augmentation ideal.
The bar differential extracts this residue:
\[
d_{\barB}[s^{-1}a \,|\, s^{-1}b]
\;=\;
\Res_{z_1 = z_2}\!\bigl[
  a(z_1)\,b(z_2)\cdot\eta_{12}
\bigr],
\qquad
\eta_{12} = d\log(z_1 - z_2).
\]
Volume~I proved $D_\cA^2 = 0$ from the Arnold relation
($\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23} + \eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{31} + \eta_{31} \wedge \eta_{12} = 0$, the fundamental identity among logarithmic $1$-forms on configuration space) and
extracted the genus tower from its curvature at higher genus.

In the \emph{topological direction}, the operators can be
separated: cut $\R$ at a point $t_*$ between $t_1$ and $t_2$.
The observables on $(-\infty, t_*)$ depend only on~$a$; the
observables on $(t_*, +\infty)$ depend only on~$b$.  The
\emph{deconcatenation coproduct} on the bar complex records this
separation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:preface2-delta}
\Delta[s^{-1}a_1|\cdots|s^{-1}a_n]
\;=\;
\sum_{i=0}^{n}
[s^{-1}a_1|\cdots|s^{-1}a_i]
\;\otimes\;
[s^{-1}a_{i+1}|\cdots|s^{-1}a_n].
\end{equation}
The $i$-th summand places $a_1, \ldots, a_i$ on the left
half-line and $a_{i+1}, \ldots, a_n$ on the right.
The two directions are independent: algebraically,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:preface2-coderivation}
\Delta \circ D_\cA
\;=\;
(D_\cA \otimes \id \;+\; \id \otimes D_\cA) \circ \Delta.
\end{equation}
The differential is a \emph{coderivation} of the coproduct.

The two directions arise because the bar complex gives a formal bar/Koszul model of a Lagrangian self-intersection $\cL \times_{\mathscr{M}}^h \cL$ in a $(-2)$-shifted symplectic stack $\mathscr{M}$ (in the sense of Pantev--To\"en--Vaqui\'e--Vezzosi: a closed $2$-form of cohomological degree $-2$ on the derived moduli stack, inducing a non-degenerate pairing on the tangent complex) (Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-is-self-intersection}).  The holomorphic direction is the Koszul differential of the Lagrangian embedding; the topological direction is the groupoid diagonal of the self-intersection.  Their independence---the coderivation property---is the statement that the diagonal is a groupoid morphism.  Nothing is imposed; both structures are inherited from the single geometric fact that the bar complex is computing a derived fiber product.  The chiral algebra fixes a formal local bulk model: the HKR theorem for Lagrangian embeddings recovers the shifted-cotangent side $T^*[-1]\cL_\cA$ from the boundary data, while the actual ambient $(-2)$-shifted symplectic stack still requires the extra Darboux $1$-form (Theorem~\ref{thm:holographic-reconstruction}).

\section*{II.\quad The Heisenberg on $\C \times \R$}

Take $\cA = \cH_k$: one generator $J$, one OPE
$J(z)\,J(w) \sim k/(z-w)^2$.  Two operators sit at $(z_1, t_1)$
and $(z_2, t_2)$ with $t_1 < t_2$.  Their bar element is
$[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The holomorphic direction.}\enspace
The bar differential is the codimension-$1$ boundary operator
on $\FM_k(\C)$, the Fulton--MacPherson compactification (the iterated real blowup of $\C^k$ along all diagonal strata).  For two insertions, the boundary stratum
$E_{12} \cong S^1$ of the FM blowup extracts the mode products
$J_{(n)}J$ from the OPE.  The only nonvanishing mode is
$J_{(1)}J = k$ (the level), so
\[
d_{\barB}[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
\;=\;
J_{(1)}J
\;=\; k.
\]
There is no simple-pole OPE ($J_{(0)}J = 0$), so the bracket
component of the bar differential vanishes.  The entire
holomorphic interaction is the scalar~$k$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The topological direction.}\enspace
Cut $\R$ at each possible gap:
\begin{align*}
\Delta[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
&\;=\;
[\,] \otimes [s^{-1}J | s^{-1}J]
&&\text{(cut before $t_1$)}
\\
&\;+\;
[s^{-1}J] \otimes [s^{-1}J]
&&\text{(cut between $t_1$ and $t_2$)}
\\
&\;+\;
[s^{-1}J | s^{-1}J] \otimes [\,]
&&\text{(cut after $t_2$)}.
\end{align*}
The middle term places one field on each half-line.  Since
$J_{(0)}J = 0$, the two separated fields do not interact: the
factorisation is exact.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The coderivation property.}\enspace
Apply $D_\cA$ to the left-hand side:
$D_\cA[s^{-1}J | s^{-1}J] = k$, then
$\Delta(k) = k \otimes [\,] + [\,] \otimes k$.
Apply $(D_\cA \otimes \id + \id \otimes D_\cA)$ to the
right-hand side: only the first and third summands contribute
(the middle term $[s^{-1}J] \otimes [s^{-1}J]$ gives zero because
$D_\cA(s^{-1}J) = 0$), producing $[\,] \otimes k + k \otimes [\,]$.
Both sides agree.  The collision in $\C$ and the cut in $\R$
commute.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The configuration-space product.}\enspace
The holomorphic collision lives on $\FM_2(\C)$.  The topological
cut lives on $\Conf_2^<(\R) = \{t_1 < t_2\} \cong \R_{>0}$,
which is contractible.  The bar element
$[s^{-1}J | s^{-1}J]$ is parametrised by the product
\[
\FM_2(\C) \times \Conf_2^<(\R).
\]
The differential sees the first factor.  The coproduct sees the
second.  The coderivation property is the independence of the two
factors.  The same decomposition holds at degree~$n$:
\[
\bar{B}^{\mathrm{ch},n}(\cA)
\;\subset\;
(s^{-1}\bar\cA)^{\otimes n}
\otimes
\Omega^\bullet\bigl(\FM_n(\C) \times \Conf_n^<(\R)\bigr).
\]
The ordered configuration space $\Conf_n^<(\R) \cong
\R^{n-1}_{> 0}$ is contractible; its $E_1$-coalgebra structure
contracts to the strict coassociative coproduct---deconcatenation.
No higher homotopies survive.  The coproduct is the unique
coassociative coproduct on $T^c(V)$, determined by the
cogenerators.


\medskip\noindent\textbf{The Feynman transform and 't~Hooft expansion.}\enspace
Volume~I organises every genus-tower structure through the
convolution principle: the modular convolution dg Lie algebra
$\gAmod := \Hom_\Sigma(F\!\Com,\, \End_{\barB(\cA)})$
carries a dg Lie bracket from operadic composition, and
its universal MC element
$\Theta_\cA := D_\cA - d_0$ encodes the full genus tower.
Here $F\!\Com$ is the Feynman transform of the commutative
modular operad $\Com$ (with $\Com(g,n) = \Bbbk$ for all stable $(g,n)$), generated by stable graphs with edge-contraction
differential ($D^2 = 0$ from $\partial^2 = 0$ on
$\overline{\cM}_{g,n}$).

The associative variant
${\gAmod}^{E_1} := \Hom_\Sigma(F\!\Ass,\,
\End_{\barB^{E_1}(\cA)})$
($F\!\Ass$ is the Feynman transform of the associative modular operad, summing over ribbon graphs rather than stable graphs)
replaces stable graphs by \emph{ribbon graphs} (fatgraphs)
and is the algebraic form of the 't~Hooft $1/N$ expansion:
$\hbar = 1/N^2$, the MC element is
$\sum_g N^{2-2g}F_g(\lambda)$, planar diagrams dominate at
large~$N$, and the coinvariant projection
$\Theta^{E_1} \mapsto \Theta$ is the passage from
colour-ordered to full amplitudes.

Volume~II enriches this from a one-coloured to a two-coloured
convolution algebra: the Swiss-cheese operad $\SCchtop$
couples the $\C$-direction (Feynman transform, holomorphic)
to the $\R$-direction (coproduct, topological), and the
MC element $\alpha_T \in \MC(\gSC_T)$ encodes both.

\section*{III.\quad The Swiss-cheese operad $\SCchtop$}

The product $\FM_k(\C) \times \Conf_k^<(\R)$ is the space of
operations of a two-coloured operad:
\begin{alignat*}{2}
&\text{closed-to-closed:}\quad
&&\SCchtop(\mathsf{ch}^k;\,\mathsf{ch})
\;=\; \FM_k(\C), \\
&\text{mixed-to-open:}\quad
&&\SCchtop(\mathsf{ch}^k, \mathsf{top}^m;\,\mathsf{top})
\;=\; \FM_k(\C) \times E_1(m), \\
&\text{open-to-closed:}\quad
&&\SCchtop(\ldots, \mathsf{top}, \ldots;\,\mathsf{ch})
\;=\; \varnothing.
\end{alignat*}
Closed colour: holomorphic collisions on $\FM_k(\C)$.  Open colour:
topological orderings on $E_1(m) = \Conf_m^<(\R)$.  Mixed: the
product.  Composition is FM substitution on the closed factor,
interval insertion on the open factor.  The empty open-to-closed
component is directionality: bulk restricts to boundary, not
conversely.

The bar complex $(\barBch(\cA), D_\cA, \Delta)$ is a coalgebra
over $\SCchtop$: the differential is the closed-colour
co-structure and the coproduct is the open-colour co-structure.
By the Koszul self-duality $(\SCchtop)^! \simeq \SCchtop$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:SC-self-duality}), the dual $\cA^!$ inherits
an $\SCchtop$-\emph{algebra} structure.  The verification
for the Heisenberg algebra is the four-step proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:rosetta-swiss-cheese}, carried out in the Rosetta
Stone chapter.

The closed colour is the Koszul direction (holomorphic collisions on~$\C$); the open colour is the groupoid direction (topological cuts along~$\R$).  Directionality---open-to-closed operations are empty---reflects the Springer pattern: representations restrict from $G$ to $B$ but not conversely.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Homotopy-Koszulity.}\enspace
The classical Swiss-cheese operad $\mathsf{SC}$ (with $E_2$ on the
closed colour and $E_1$ on the open colour) is Koszul (Livernet).
Kontsevich formality---the fact that $C_*(\FM_k(\C))$ is formal as a dg operad, i.e., quasi-isomorphic to its cohomology $H_*(\FM_k(\C)) \cong E_2(k)$ (the homology operad of the little-$2$-disks operad, governing Gerstenhaber algebras)---provides a quasi-isomorphism
$\SCchtop \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathsf{SC}$.  Bar-cobar transfer
gives
\[
\Omega\mathbf{B}(\SCchtop) \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \SCchtop.
\]
The bar-cobar adjunction on $\SCchtop$-algebras is a Quillen
equivalence (Theorem~\ref{thm:homotopy-Koszul}).


\section*{IV.\quad The $\Ainf$ chiral operations}

The bar differential $D_\cA$ is a coderivation of the cofree
coalgebra $T^c(s^{-1}\bar\cA)$.  A coderivation on a cofree
coalgebra is determined by its cogenerator projections: a sequence
of multilinear maps
\[
m_k \colon A^{\otimes k} \longrightarrow
A((\lambda_1))\cdots((\lambda_{k-1})),
\qquad k \ge 1, \quad \deg m_k = 1 - k,
\]
where $\lambda_j = z_j - z_k$ are spectral parameters recording
holomorphic separations.  The identity $D_\cA^2 = 0$ expands into
the $\Ainf$ Stasheff relations: for each~$n$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:preface2-stasheff}
\sum_{\substack{i+j = n+1 \\ i,j \ge 1}}\;\sum_{s=0}^{n-j}
\pm\; m_i\bigl(a_1, \ldots, m_j(a_{s+1}, \ldots, a_{s+j}),
\ldots, a_n\bigr) \;=\; 0,
\end{equation}
with spectral substitution $\lambda_s \mapsto \Lambda_I$ when a
consecutive block $I$ collapses.

Each relation is a Stokes theorem on $\FM_n(\C)$.

\subsection*{The $n = 2$ relation: $m_1^2 = 0$}

$\FM_2(\C) = \{(z_1, z_2) : z_1 \ne z_2\}/\text{translation}$
is a point (after modding out translation, the single parameter
$z_{12} = z_1 - z_2$ is fixed by scaling).  The boundary
$\partial\FM_2(\C)$ is a single collision point $z_1 = z_2$.
The Stasheff relation at $n = 2$ is
\[
m_1(m_1(a)) \;=\; 0,
\]
which says that $m_1 = Q$ is a differential: $Q^2 = 0$.  This is
the BRST condition.

\subsection*{The $n = 3$ relation: the homotopy-associativity
identity}

The Stasheff associahedron $K_3$ is a closed interval $[0,1]$,
the quotient of $\FM_3(\C)$ by the affine group.
Its two endpoints are the two maximal collision strata:
$D_{12}=\{z_1\to z_2\}$ at one end,
$D_{23}=\{z_2\to z_3\}$ at the other.
The $n=2$ and $n=3$ Stasheff relations encode
two distinct algebraic statements:
\[
\underbrace{m_1(m_2(a,b)) + m_2(m_1(a),b) +
(-1)^{|a|} m_2(a,m_1(b))}_{n=2:\;\text{Leibniz rule}}
\;=\; 0,
\]
\[
\underbrace{m_2(m_2(a,b),c) - m_2(a,m_2(b,c))}_{%
  \text{associativity defect}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{m_1(m_3(a,b,c)) + m_3(m_1(a),b,c)
\pm\cdots}_{n=3:\;m_3\text{~interpolates}}\,.
\]
The first says $m_2$ is a chain map; the second says
$m_2$ is associative up to the homotopy~$m_3$.
The operation $m_3$ is the integral over the interval $K_3$:
it interpolates between the two boundary contributions
$D_{12}$ and $D_{23}$.  When $m_3=0$ (the Heisenberg, the free
fermion), the relation reduces to strict associativity and the
Leibniz rule.  When $m_3\ne 0$ (Kac--Moody, Virasoro), the
homotopy carries genuine geometric content: it is the Jacobiator
on $K_3$, and its nonvanishing is the failure of the OPE
to be strictly associative.

\subsection*{Higher $n$: the full Stasheff hierarchy}

At $n=4$, the associahedron $K_4$ is a compact polygon
(the Stasheff pentagon, five boundary faces from the
collision strata $D_{12}, D_{23}, D_{34}, D_{123}, D_{234}$).
The Stasheff relation involves $m_2$, $m_3$, and $m_4$,
with $m_4$ arising as the integral over the interior of~$K_4$.  For the
$\mathcal{W}_3$ algebra, $m_4$ is nonzero and encodes the quartic
contact interaction with coefficient $32/(22+5c)$.

The general pattern: $\FM_n(\C)$ is a compact manifold with
corners of real dimension $2n - 4$.  Its boundary strata are
indexed by all possible collision patterns (subsets
$S \subset \{1, \ldots, n\}$ with $|S| \ge 2$).  Each stratum
$D_S$ contributes $m_{|S|}$ composed with $m_{n - |S| + 1}$ to
the Stasheff relation, and $m_n$ itself is the integral over the
top stratum of $\FM_n(\C)$.  The identity $\partial^2 = 0$ on
$\FM_n(\C)$---every codimension-$2$ face is reached by exactly two
codimension-$1$ faces with opposite orientations---is the
combinatorial identity that makes $D_\cA^2 = 0$ hold, and hence
that makes the $\Ainf$ Stasheff relations consistent.


\section*{IV$'$.\quad The resolvent principle}

The $\Ainf$ operations $\{m_k\}_{k \ge 2}$ are determined by
a single datum: the binary product~$m_2$.  The higher
operations are not independent---they are the unique solution
of a fixed-point equation.

Let $(H, p, \iota, h)$ be a deformation retract of $(\cA, m_1)$
onto cohomology $H = H^\bullet(\cA, m_1)$, with $h^2 = h\iota =
ph = 0$.  Define the \emph{dressed propagator}
$\Phi \colon T^+(H) \to \cA$ by
\[
\Phi \;=\; \iota \;+\; h \circ m_2 \circ \Phi^{\otimes 2}.
\]
This is the \emph{tree-level bootstrap equation}: the full
propagator from boundary data~$H$ into the bulk algebra~$\cA$ is
determined self-consistently from the free embedding~$\iota$ and
the interaction vertex~$m_2$.  Its solution by iteration gives the
\emph{tree formula}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:resolvent-principle}):
\[
m_k^H
\;=\;
\sum_{T \in \mathrm{PRT}_k}
p \circ \mathfrak{m}(T) \circ \iota^{\otimes k},
\]
where $\mathrm{PRT}_k$ is the set of planar rooted binary trees
with $k$~leaves ($|\mathrm{PRT}_k| = C_{k-1}$, the Catalan
number), and $\mathfrak{m}(T)$ assigns $m_2$ to each internal
vertex and~$h$ to each internal edge.

One equation, one binary input, Catalan many trees.  The
Stasheff relations are the consistency of the iteration;
the tree formula is the perturbative expansion of
the Lagrangian self-intersection.  Wheel diagrams in the Virasoro
(Section~VII) are evaluations of this tree sum using the BRST
homotopy of Drinfeld--Sokolov reduction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:tree-wheel-bridge}).


\section*{V.\quad First computation: the Heisenberg}

\noindent\textbf{Operations.}\enspace
$m_1 = 0$ (no internal differential).
$m_2(J,J;\lambda) = k\lambda$ (the double-pole residue).
$m_k = 0$ for $k \ge 3$ (no composites, no higher poles).
The algebra is formal: the $\Ainf$ structure is concentrated in
$m_2$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{PVA on cohomology.}\enspace
On $H^\bullet(\cH_k) = \C[J]$, the binary operation decomposes
into a commutative product and a $\lambda$-bracket:
\[
\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda.
\]
This is a Poisson vertex algebra (PVA) of rank~$1$: a commutative differential algebra equipped with a $\lambda$-bracket $\{a_\lambda b\}$ satisfying sesquilinearity, skew-symmetry, the Jacobi identity, and the Leibniz rule.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Classical $r$-matrix.}\enspace
The formal Laplace transform of the $\lambda$-bracket (the algebraic correspondence $\lambda^n/n! \leftrightarrow 1/z^{n+1}$; the integral representation below is heuristic) gives the
classical $r$-matrix:
\[
r(z) \;=\;
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda z}\{J{}_\lambda J\}\,d\lambda
\;=\;
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda z}\,k\lambda\,d\lambda
\;=\; \frac{k}{z^2}.
\]
The quantum $R$-matrix: $R(z) = \exp(k\hbar/z)$.  Yang--Baxter
holds trivially (the exponential of a scalar commutes with itself).

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Line operators.}\enspace
The Koszul dual is $\cH_k^! = \cH_{-k}$ (Vol~I: level inversion,
not $k \mapsto 1/k$).  Line operators are $\cH_{-k}$-modules:
the category of Fock spaces $\mathcal{F}_\mu$, $\mu \in \C$,
semisimple.  The braiding on Fock modules is
$R_{\mu_1,\mu_2}(z) = \exp(k\mu_1\mu_2\hbar/z)$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Bulk-boundary-line.}\enspace
$\Abulk \simeq \Zder(\cH_k) = \cH_k$ (abelian algebras are their
own derived centres).  The triangle collapses:
$\cH_k \simeq \Zder(\cH_{-k}) \simeq \HH^\bullet(\cH_{-k})$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Genus~$1$.}\enspace
The bar differential acquires curvature $d^2 = k \cdot \omega_1$
from the self-loop graph $\Gamma_{\mathrm{irr}}$ on
$\overline{\cM}_{1,1}$.  The modular characteristic is
$\kappa(\cH_k) = k$: the same scalar that governs the OPE, the
$\lambda$-bracket, the $R$-matrix, and the genus-$1$ obstruction.
The free energy $F_1 = -(k/24)\log\eta(\tau)$.


\section*{VI.\quad Second computation: Kac--Moody at level~$k$}

\noindent\textbf{Operations.}\enspace
$m_1 = 0$.
$m_2(J^a, J^b;\lambda) = f^{ab}{}_c\,J^c + k\,\delta^{ab}\lambda$
(the simple-pole bracket plus the double-pole metric).
$m_3 \ne 0$: the Jacobiator homotopy on $\FM_3(\C)$.  The three
codimension-$1$ boundary strata of $\FM_3(\C)$ contribute the
three terms $m_2(m_2(-,-),-)$ in the Stasheff identity; their
failure to cancel is the Jacobi identity defect, corrected by
$m_3$.  Higher $m_k = 0$ by dimension counting on $\FM_k(\C)$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{PVA on cohomology.}\enspace
$\{J^a_\lambda J^b\} = f^{ab}{}_c\,J^c + k\,\delta^{ab}\lambda$.
This is the affine PVA at level~$k$.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Classical $r$-matrix.}\enspace
The Laplace transform of the $\lambda$-bracket gives the
position-space OPE kernel:
\[
\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda z}\,
  \{J^a{}_\lambda J^b\}\, d\lambda
\;\otimes\; J_a \otimes J_b
\;=\;
\frac{f^{ab}{}_c\,J^c}{z}\,J_a\otimes J_b
  + \frac{k\,\delta^{ab}}{z^2}\,J_a\otimes J_b
\;=\;
\frac{\Omega}{z} + \frac{k\,K_{\fg}}{z^2},
\]
where $\Omega = \sum_a J^a \otimes J_a$ is the split Casimir
and $K_{\fg} = \sum_a J_a \otimes J^a$ is the Killing form
tensor.  The scalar term $k\,K_{\fg}/z^2$ is ad-invariant
and contributes trivially to the CYBE; the collision residue
r-matrix is $r(z) = \Omega/z$.
The KZ connection $\nabla_{\mathrm{KZ}} = d - \hbar\,\Omega/z$ is
the flat connection on conformal blocks whose monodromy is the
quantum $R$-matrix of $U_\hbar(\fg)$.  The CYBE for $r(z) =
\Omega/z$ is the Lie-algebra Jacobi identity for $\fg$: the
Arnold relation on $\FM_3(\C)$ applied to the affine OPE.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Line operators.}\enspace
$\cA^! = \widehat{\fg}_{-k-2h^\vee}$ (Feigin--Frenkel).  Line
operators are modules for the dual algebra: category~$\mathcal{O}$
at the dual level.  Drinfeld--Kohno duality identifies the braided
module category with the quantum group category.

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Genus~$1$.}\enspace
$\kappa(\widehat{\fg}_k) = \dim\fg\,(k + h^\vee)/(2h^\vee)$.
Anti-symmetry: $\kappa_k + \kappa_{-k-2h^\vee} = 0$.


\section*{VII.\quad Third computation: $\mathcal{W}_3$ and
the birth of nonlinearity}

The $\mathcal{W}_3$ algebra is the first family where the $\Ainf$
structure is genuinely infinite.

The $\mathcal{W}_3$ algebra has two generators: the stress tensor
$T$ of weight~$2$ and a spin-$3$ current $W$ of weight~$3$.  The
$TT$ OPE has poles up to order~$4$; the $TW$ OPE up to order~$3$;
and the $WW$ OPE up to order~$6$, with a composite field
\[
\Lambda \;=\; :(TT): \;-\; \tfrac{3}{10}\,\partial^2 T
\]
of weight~$4$ appearing at the fourth-order pole.  The coefficient
of $\Lambda$ in the $WW$ OPE is $16/(22 + 5c)$: it diverges at
$c = -22/5$ (the Lee--Yang value).

\smallskip
\noindent\textbf{Operations.}\enspace
$m_2(T,T;\lambda) = (c/2)\lambda^3 + 2T\lambda + \partial T$
(the full $TT$ OPE in spectral parametrisation).
The $WW$ operation is
\[
m_2(W,W;\lambda) \;=\; \frac{c}{360}\lambda^5
+ \frac{1}{3}T\lambda^3
+ \frac{1}{2}(\partial T)\lambda^2
+ \Bigl(\frac{3}{10}\partial^2 T + \frac{32}{5c}\,T^2\Bigr)\lambda
+ \frac{1}{15}\partial^3 T + \frac{32}{5c}\,T\partial T,
\]

\section*{IX.\quad Koszulness}

The prototype for Koszul duality as convolution is the Chriss--Ginzburg theorem: the Borel--Moore homology of the Steinberg variety $\widetilde{\mathcal{N}} \times_{\fg} \widetilde{\mathcal{N}}$ (where $\widetilde{\mathcal{N}}$ is the Springer resolution of the nilpotent cone) recovers the Hecke algebra $H_W$ of the Weyl group:
$H_*^{BM}(\widetilde{\mathcal{N}} \times_{\fg}
\widetilde{\mathcal{N}}) \cong H_W$.  The Hecke algebra is
a convolution algebra.  The bar complex $\barBch(\cA)$ on
$\FM_n(\C) \times \Conf_n^{<}(\R)$ is the convolution algebra
of chiral algebra: the bar differential is convolution on
$\FM_n(\C)$; the coproduct is restriction on $\Conf_n^{<}(\R)$;
the six projections recover the algebra, the Koszul dual, the line
category, the $R$-matrix, the genus tower, the PVA\@.
For the affine lineage, the convolution produces the quantum group:
$\cC_{\mathrm{line}}^{\mathrm{red}}|_{\mathrm{eval}} \simeq
\mathrm{Rep}_q(\fg)$ and the bar complex computes the Jones
polynomial (Theorem~\ref{thm:affine-monodromy-identification},
Corollary~\ref{cor:jones-polynomial}).

\emph{Koszulness is the condition that this presentation is
concentrated.}

When the bar cohomology is concentrated ($H^{p,q}(\barBch(\cA)) = 0$
for $q \neq 0$), the derived self-intersection
$\Steinb_b := \barBch(\cA) \times_\cA^h \barBch(\cA)$
is \emph{clean}: the excess Tor sheaf
$\cT\!\mathit{or}^{\cO_\cA}_q(\cO_{\barBch},\cO_{\barBch})$
vanishes for $q \neq 0$, the Borel--Moore homology is
one-dimensional per stratum, and every invariant projects as
a scalar.  Non-concentration means excess $\cT\!\mathit{or}_q
\neq 0$: the self-intersection carries higher derived structure,
$\Ainf$ operations proliferate, and curvature and braiding
entangle.  In this sense, chiral Koszulness is a transversality
condition on the Lagrangian self-intersection.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The bar complex as a bigraded object.}\enspace
The bar complex carries two gradings: the \emph{internal degree}
(cohomological degree within~$\cA$) and the \emph{bar degree}
(number of tensor factors minus one).  The bar differential mixes
these gradings: it lowers bar degree by one and raises internal degree
by the pole order of the OPE.  The resulting spectral sequence---the
PBW spectral sequence of Volume~I---converges to the bar cohomology.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The concentration condition.}\enspace
The bar cohomology $H^{p,q}(\barBch(\cA))$ is bigraded.  Say $\cA$
is \emph{chirally Koszul} if this cohomology is concentrated in a
single line:
\[
H^{p,q}\bigl(\barBch(\cA)\bigr) = 0 \quad \text{for } q \neq 0.
\]
This is a single condition.  Twelve consequences radiate from it,
each illuminating a different face of the same concentration.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The twelve faces.}\enspace
The Koszul dodecahedron of Volume~I:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2em,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Concentration}: bar cohomology in one degree.
\item \emph{Spectral collapse}: the PBW spectral sequence degenerates
  at~$E_2$.  The associated graded $\gr_F \cA$ is classically Koszul
  (Priddy--BGS), and the chiral deformation introduces no corrections.
\item \emph{Formality}: $m_k = 0$ for $k \geq 3$.  One-loop
  exactness: multi-particle collisions on $\FM_k(\C)$ contribute no
  new cohomology beyond what the pairwise OPE already sees.
\item \emph{Small resolution}: the bar-cobar counit
  $\Omegach(\barBch(\cA)) \xrightarrow{\sim} \cA$ is a
  quasi-isomorphism.  The \emph{big} resolution $\cA^!_\infty :=
  \mathbb{D}_{\operatorname{Ran}} \barBch(\cA)$---the Verdier dual
  of the bar coalgebra---exists unconditionally for every~$\cA$.  On
  the Koszul locus, big $=$ small: $\cA^!_\infty$ is formal and its
  cohomology is~$\cA^!$.  Off the Koszul locus,
  $\cA^!_\infty$ carries genuinely higher operations.
\item \emph{Ext diagonal}: $\Ext^{p,q}_\cA(\omega_X, \omega_X) = 0$
  for $p \neq q$ (the BGS criterion chiralized).
\item \emph{Hochschild concentration}:
  $\mathrm{ChirHoch}^n(\cA) = 0$ for $n \notin \{0, 1, 2\}$.
\item \emph{Factorization homology}: $\int_{\Sigma_g}\cA$ is
  concentrated in degree~$0$ for all genera.
\item \emph{Barr--Beck--Lurie}: the comparison functor for the
  bar-cobar monad is an equivalence on the fiber; abstract monadicity
  applies without correction.
\item \emph{Kac--Shapovalov regularity}: $\det G_h \neq 0$ in the
  bar-relevant range; no singular vectors intrude to create unexpected
  cohomology classes.
\item \emph{Stratum acyclicity}: restriction to every FM boundary
  stratum is acyclic outside degree~$0$.
\item \emph{Lagrangian transversality}: the deformation and
  obstruction complexes $\mathbf{Q}_g(\cA)$ and $\mathbf{Q}_g(\cA^!)$
  are complementary Lagrangians
  (Vol~I, Theorem~C) (conditional on perfectness).
\item \emph{Hodge purity}: $\barB_n(\cA)$ is pure of weight~$n$
  as a mixed Hodge module on $\FM_n(X)$ (forward implication proved; converse open).
\end{enumerate}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Classical specialization.}\enspace
When $X = \mathrm{pt}$, the chiral tensor product is the ordinary
tensor product, the FM compactification is the Stasheff
associahedron, and all twelve criteria collapse to the classical
Koszul theory of Priddy--BGG--BGS--Loday--Vallette.  The chiral theory
is not a generalization that weakens these conditions; it is a
\emph{lift} that reveals their geometric origin.  Concentration of
bar cohomology, in the classical setting, is a combinatorial miracle.
In the chiral setting, it is a consequence of Arnold cancellation on
FM compactifications: the boundary strata produce exact sequences,
and Koszulness is the statement that these exact sequences have no
higher cohomology.  The ``miracle'' of classical Koszulness is the
shadow of the geometry of configuration spaces.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The examples.}\enspace
The Heisenberg is Koszul: $m_k = 0$ for $k \geq 3$
(Section~V), the PBW spectral sequence has nothing to degenerate, and
$\cH_k^! = \cH_{-k}$ is the level-inverted Heisenberg.

Kac--Moody $\widehat{\fg}_k$ is Koszul: the PBW spectral sequence
degenerates at~$E_2$ because $\gr_F \widehat{\fg}_k$ is a polynomial
algebra (classically Koszul by Priddy).  The Koszul dual is
$\widehat{\fg}_{-k-2h^\vee}$ at the dual level.  The big and small
resolutions agree.

Virasoro is chirally Koszul (the PBW spectral sequence concentrates),
but its Swiss-cheese structure is \emph{non-formal}: the transferred
operations $m_k^{\mathrm{SC}} \neq 0$ for all $k \geq 3$
(Section~VII).  The big resolution $(\mathrm{Vir}_c)^!_\infty$
carries the infinite tower of wheel diagrams; the small Koszul dual
$\mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}$ is its shadow.  The boundary between formal
and non-formal Swiss-cheese structure is controlled by the pole
structure of the OPE: simple poles give formal algebras;
higher-order poles create the higher operations that break formality.

Shadow depth and Koszulness are independent invariants.
Four depth classes emerge, each with a distinct
self-intersection geometry
(\S\ref{sec:shadow-tower-atlas}): class~$\mathbf{G}$ (clean
intersection, depth~$2$: Heisenberg, lattice VOAs),
class~$\mathbf{L}$ (Lie-transverse, depth~$3$: affine
$V_k(\fg)$, where Jacobi kills the quartic),
class~$\mathbf{C}$ (contact, depth~$4$: $\beta\gamma$), and
class~$\mathbf{M}$ (infinite excess tower, depth~$\infty$:
Virasoro, $\cW_N$).  Depth classifies the \emph{complexity}
of the shadow tower, not the existence of a small resolution.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Homotopy-Koszulity of the operad.}\enspace
Individual algebras may have formal or non-formal
Swiss-cheese structure; the \emph{operad} $\SCchtop$
is always homotopy-Koszul.  Homotopy-Koszulity
(Theorem~\ref{thm:homotopy-Koszul}) means the bar-cobar adjunction
on $\SCchtop$-algebras is a Quillen equivalence---a property of the
categorical framework, not of any particular algebra.  It is what
makes the 3d MC element~$\alpha_T$ of Section~VIII well-defined:
$\mathbf{B}(\SCchtop)$ has $\partial^2 = 0$ because the operad is
homotopy-Koszul, and the six projections of~$\alpha_T$ exist because
the bar-cobar resolution is a resolution.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Koszulness and PVA descent.}\enspace
On the Koszul locus (formality, criterion~(iii)), the cohomological
shadow of the bar complex is exact: the higher $m_k$ vanish on
cohomology, and $m_2$ alone survives.  The PVA structure of the
next section is therefore the \emph{complete} invariant of a Koszul
chiral algebra at the cohomological level.  For algebras with
non-formal Swiss-cheese structure
(Virasoro, $\mathcal{W}_3$), the PVA is only the leading term of an
infinite tower: the higher transferred $m_k$ carry information that the PVA does
not see, and quantization must lift the entire tower, not just the
PVA bracket.


\section*{X.\quad PVA descent}
\label{sec:preface-pva-descent}

The $\Ainf$ operations $m_k$ are chain-level data.  On cohomology
$H^\bullet(A, Q)$, the higher operations $m_{k \ge 3}$ vanish (they
are $Q$-exact by the homotopy-transfer argument: each $m_k$ for
$k \ge 3$ arises from a chain of dimension $\ge 1$ on
$\Conf_k^<(\R)$, which is contractible, so $m_k = Q(h_{k-1})$).
The binary operation $m_2$ survives, because its topological chain
is zero-dimensional (a point in a contractible space).

\subsection*{Decomposition of $m_2$}

The surviving operation $m_2(a,b;\zeta)$ is a formal Laurent
series in the collision variable $\zeta = z_1 - z_2$.  Decompose
it into regular and singular parts:
\[
m_2(a,b;\zeta)
\;=\;
\underbrace{m_2^{\mathrm{reg}}(a,b;\zeta)}_{\text{no pole at }
\zeta = 0}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\sum_{n \ge 0}
\frac{a_{(n)}b}{\zeta^{n+1}}}_{\text{singular: the OPE}}.
\]
The regular part at $\zeta = 0$ is a commutative product:
\[
\mu(a,b) \;:=\; m_2^{\mathrm{reg}}(a,b;0).
\]
The singular part, after Borel transform
$\zeta^{-n-1} \mapsto \lambda^{(n)}/n!$, is a $\lambda$-bracket:
\[
\{a_\lambda b\}
\;:=\;
\sum_{n \ge 0} \frac{\lambda^{(n)}}{n!}\,a_{(n)}b.
\]
Together, $(\mu, \{-_\lambda-\})$ form a $(-1)$-shifted Poisson
vertex algebra on $H^\bullet(A, Q)$.

\subsection*{The Jacobi identity from $\FM_3(\C)$}

The Jacobi identity for $\{-_\lambda-\}$ is the $n = 3$ Stasheff
relation~\eqref{eq:preface2-stasheff} projected to cohomology.
Write it out.  The boundary $\partial\FM_3(\C)$ has three
codimension-$1$ strata:
\[
D_{12} \;=\; \{z_1 \to z_2\}, \qquad
D_{23} \;=\; \{z_2 \to z_3\}, \qquad
D_{13} \;=\; \{z_1 \to z_3\}.
\]
Each stratum contributes one term to the $n = 3$ relation:
\begin{align*}
D_{12}&\colon\quad
m_2\bigl(m_2(a,b;\zeta_{12}),\, c;\, \zeta_{13}\bigr),
\\
D_{23}&\colon\quad
m_2\bigl(a,\, m_2(b,c;\zeta_{23});\, \zeta_{12}\bigr),
\\
D_{13}&\colon\quad
-\,m_2\bigl(m_2(a,c;\zeta_{13}),\, b;\, \zeta_{12}\bigr).
\end{align*}
The sign on $D_{13}$ is the Koszul sign from permuting $b$ past
$c$.  Stokes' theorem on $\FM_3(\C)$ gives
$\sum_S \Res_{D_S}(\omega) = 0$ for any closed form~$\omega$ on
$\FM_3(\C)$.  Applying this to the form built from three OPE
kernels, projecting to the singular parts, and performing the Borel
transform gives the $\lambda$-bracket Jacobi identity:
\[
\{a_\lambda \{b_\mu c\}\}
\;-\;
\{b_\mu \{a_\lambda c\}\}
\;=\;
\{\{a_\lambda b\}_{\lambda+\mu} c\}.
\]
The three boundary faces of $\FM_3(\C)$ are three collisions; the
cancellation of the integral over $\partial\FM_3(\C)$ is the Jacobi
identity.

\subsection*{Leibniz, skew-symmetry, and the exchange cylinder}

Leibniz ($\{a_\lambda bc\} = \{a_\lambda b\}c +
b\{a_\lambda c\}$) follows from the factorisation of the regular
part~$\mu$ through the collision stratum: when $z_2 \to z_3$ while
$z_1$ is far away, $m_2(a, m_2^{\mathrm{reg}}(b,c;0); \zeta_{12})$
decomposes into the product of $m_2(a,b;\zeta_{12})$ with~$c$ plus
$b$ with $m_2(a,c;\zeta_{13})$.

Skew-symmetry ($\{a_\lambda b\} = -\{b_{-\lambda-\partial} a\}$)
follows from the exchange automorphism $z_1 \leftrightarrow z_2$ on
$\FM_2(\C)$, which reverses the sign of $\zeta = z_1 - z_2$ and
introduces $\partial$ from the Jacobian of the coordinate change.

\subsection*{Heisenberg and Kac--Moody PVAs}

For the Heisenberg: $m_2^{\mathrm{reg}} = 0$ (no regular part), so
$\mu = 0$ and the product is trivial.  The $\lambda$-bracket
$\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$ is the entire PVA\@.  Jacobi is
vacuous (single generator).

For Kac--Moody: $m_2^{\mathrm{reg}}(J^a, J^b; 0) = 0$
(the structure constants are in the singular part).  The
$\lambda$-bracket $\{J^a_\lambda J^b\} = f^{ab}{}_c J^c +
k\delta^{ab}\lambda$ recovers the affine PVA\@.  Jacobi is the
Lie-algebra Jacobi identity for $f^{ab}{}_c$, which is indeed the
residue of the three-face Stokes identity on $\FM_3(\C)$ evaluated
on the $\widehat{\fg}_k$ OPE.


\section*{XI.\quad The bulk-boundary-line triangle}

A holomorphic--topological theory on $\C \times \R_{\ge 0}$
produces three algebraic objects: bulk local operators $\Abulk$,
boundary local operators $\Bbound$, and line operators
$\cC_{\mathrm{line}}$.

\subsection*{The corrected triangle}

The Koszul triangle is
\[
\Abulk
\;\simeq\;
\Zder(\Bbound)
\;\simeq\;
\Zder(\cC_{\mathrm{line}})
\;\simeq\;
\HH^\bullet_{\mathrm{ch}}(\cA^!),
\qquad
\cC_{\mathrm{line}} \simeq \cA^!\text{-mod}.
\]
Line operators are modules for the Koszul dual~$\cA^!$.

\subsection*{The derived centre}

The derived centre of $\Bbound$ is
\[
\Zder(\Bbound) \;:=\;
\RHom_{\Bbound\text{-}\Bbound}(\Bbound,\,\Bbound),
\]
computed in the derived category of $\Bbound$-bimodules.
For the Heisenberg: $\Bbound = \cH_k$ is commutative
($J_{(0)}J = 0$), so $\Zder(\cH_k) = \cH_k$.
For Kac--Moody: $\Zder(\widehat{\fg}_{-h^\vee})$ recovers the Feigin--Frenkel
centre $\mathfrak{z}(\widehat{\fg}_{-h^\vee})$ at the critical level $k = -h^\vee$---the algebra of
${}^L\!G$-opers on the formal disc.

\subsection*{Lines as Koszul-dual modules}

The identification
$\cC_{\mathrm{line}} \simeq \Bbound^!\text{-mod}$ is the
open-colour projection of the Swiss-cheese structure: the open
sector of the $\SCchtop$-algebra $(\barBch(\cA), D_\cA, \Delta)$
is an $E_1$-coalgebra whose comodules are $\Bbound^!$-modules.

For the Heisenberg: $\Bbound^! = \cH_{-k}$,
$\cC_{\mathrm{line}} = $ Fock modules.  For Kac--Moody:
$\Bbound^! = \widehat{\fg}_{-k-2h^\vee}$,
$\cC_{\mathrm{line}} = $ category $\mathcal{O}$ at the dual
level.

\subsection*{The spectral $R$-matrix and Yang--Baxter}

Two parallel line operators $L_1(z_1)$, $L_2(z_2)$ on the
boundary interact through the bulk: the bulk-to-boundary OPE in
the strip between them produces a braiding
\[
R_{L_1, L_2}(z) \colon L_1 \otimes L_2
\;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\;
L_2 \otimes L_1,
\]
meromorphic in $z = z_1 - z_2$.  This braiding is computed by a
configuration integral over $\FM_2(\C)$ with $E_1$-homotopies
along the half-interval: the holomorphic collision produces the
residue, and the topological ordering produces the braiding sign.

Three lines $L_1, L_2, L_3$ at $z_1, z_2, z_3$ can be braided in
two orders, corresponding to the two maximal degenerations of
$\FM_3(\C)$.  The consistency of these two orderings---the
cancellation of the integral over $\partial\FM_3(\C)$---is the
Yang--Baxter equation
\[
R_{12}(z_{12})\,R_{13}(z_{13})\,R_{23}(z_{23})
\;=\;
R_{23}(z_{23})\,R_{13}(z_{13})\,R_{12}(z_{12}).
\]
This is the same Stokes mechanism that gives the PVA Jacobi
identity in Section~X: the $n = 3$ boundary cancellation on
$\FM_3(\C)$.  The Jacobi identity is the closed-colour projection;
the Yang--Baxter equation is the open-colour projection.  Both are
shadows of the single $\Ainf$ Stasheff identity at $n = 3$.

For the Heisenberg: $R(z) = e^{k\hbar/z}$ (scalar, YBE trivial).
For Kac--Moody: $R(z)$ is the quantum group $R$-matrix of
$U_\hbar(\fg)$, with $r(z) = \Omega/z$ as classical limit.


\section*{XII.\quad PVA quantization: from classical to quantum}

The PVA $\{-_\lambda -\}$ on $H^\bullet(A, Q)$ is the
\emph{classical} shadow of the $\Ainf$ chiral algebra.  The
quantization problem is the reverse: given a PVA, construct the
$\Ainf$ chiral algebra whose cohomological descent recovers it.

This is a lifting problem.  Start with a PVA
$(V, \cdot, \{-_\lambda -\})$.  The bracket
$\{a_\lambda b\} = \sum_{n \ge 0} \frac{\lambda^{(n)}}{n!}
a_{(n)}b$ encodes the singular part of the OPE; the product
$a \cdot b$ encodes the regular part.  Together they determine
$m_2(a,b;\lambda)$ on cohomology.  The quantization question: does
there exist an $\Ainf$ chiral algebra~$A$ with $H^\bullet(A) = V$
and $m_2|_{H^\bullet} = \mu + \{-_\lambda-\}$?

The obstruction to lifting lives in the Hochschild cohomology
$\HH^3_{\mathrm{ch}}(V)$: the failure of the Jacobi identity
to hold at the chain level, after the first-order lift, is a
cocycle whose class is the obstruction.  When the obstruction
vanishes, the lift exists and is controlled by a Maurer--Cartan
element in the chiral deformation complex
$\mathrm{Def}_{\mathrm{ch}}(V)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The Heisenberg quantization.}\enspace
The PVA $\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$ lifts to the Heisenberg
$\cH_k$ with $m_2(J,J;\lambda) = k\lambda$ and $m_{k \ge 3} = 0$.
The obstruction vanishes: $\HH^3_{\mathrm{ch}}(\C[J]) = 0$
(the PVA is freely generated).  The quantization is unique.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The Kac--Moody quantization.}\enspace
The affine PVA $\{J^a_\lambda J^b\} = f^{ab}{}_c J^c +
k\delta^{ab}\lambda$ lifts to $\widehat{\fg}_k$.  The
obstruction at $m_3$ is the Jacobiator, which is a coboundary:
$m_3$ exists and equals the homotopy-transfer correction from
$\FM_3(\C)$.  Higher obstructions vanish by dimension.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The Khan--Zeng 3d sigma model.}\enspace
Khan--Zeng construct a 3d holomorphic--topological sigma model
from any PVA target.  The boundary algebra of this theory is the
quantization of the PVA\@.  The BV-BRST complex of the sigma model
on $\C \times \R_{\ge 0}$, with the PVA as target space data,
produces the $\Ainf$ operations $m_k$ by Feynman-diagram evaluation
on $\FM_k(\C)$: each $k$-ary operation is a sum of tree Feynman
diagrams with $k$ external legs and vertices weighted by the PVA
brackets.  The Stasheff identity is Stokes' theorem on the boundary
of the Feynman-integral compactification---the same boundary
cancellation that gives the $\Ainf$ relations from $\FM_k(\C)$.

This sigma-model construction gives the physical proof of the
quantization theorem: the PVA data determine a consistent field
theory, and the field theory produces the $\Ainf$ algebra.  The
mathematical proof is the bar-cobar correspondence of
Theorem~\ref{thm:homotopy-Koszul}: the Quillen equivalence
guarantees essential uniqueness of the $\SCchtop$-algebra structure,
hence the quantization is unique up to contractible ambiguity.

\subsection*{Anomaly completion}

At genus $g \ge 1$, the bar complex is \emph{curved}: the
differential satisfies $d^2 = \kappa(\Bbound) \cdot \omega_g$,
where $\kappa(\Bbound)$ is the modular Koszul curvature
(Vol~I, Theorem~D) and $\omega_g$ is the period form of the
genus-$g$ surface.  The curvature obstructs naive factorisation:
one cannot simply restrict $\alpha_T$ to genus~$g$ without
absorbing $\kappa$ into the algebraic structure.

The correct absorption is the \emph{transgression algebra}.
The naive approach---adjoin an odd supercentral element $\eta$
with $d\eta = \Theta$---fails in characteristic zero because
$\eta$ odd and supercentral forces $\eta^2 = 0$, killing the
secondary structure that controls genus~$g \ge 2$.  The correct
construction is the \emph{skew polynomial extension}: adjoin
$\eta$ of degree~$1$ satisfying the graded-commutation rule
$\eta b = (-1)^{|b|} b \eta$ for all $b \in B$, with
$d\eta = \Theta = \kappa \cdot \omega_1$.  The element~$\eta$
is free with respect to its own powers: $\eta^2 \ne 0$.  The
resulting dg~algebra
\[
B_\Theta
\;=\;
\frac{B * \Bbbk\langle\eta\rangle}
{\langle \eta b - (-1)^{|b|} b\eta \rangle},
\qquad
d\eta = \Theta,
\]
is the \emph{transgression algebra} of $(B, \Theta)$.  A
$B_\Theta$-module is a $B$-module equipped with a
\emph{neutralisation}: a degree-$1$ $B$-linear endomorphism
$h_M$ satisfying $dh_M = \Theta_M$.  The neutralisation is
the chain-level homotopy that resolves the curvature
obstruction on $M$---the datum needed to extend the genus-$0$
module structure through genus~$1$.

The \emph{secondary anomaly} $u := \eta^2$ is closed and
central in $B_\Theta$.  It is the genus-raising operation: the
\emph{genus-$g$ Clifford completion}
$\mathfrak G_g(B_\Theta)$ adjoins handle operators
$\alpha_1, \beta_1, \ldots, \alpha_g, \beta_g$ (modelling the $a$-cycles and $b$-cycles of~$\Sigma_g$) with the
Clifford relation
$\alpha_i \beta_j + \beta_j \alpha_i = \delta_{ij} \eta^2$
and $\alpha_i^2 = \beta_j^2 = 0$ (encoding the intersection form on $H_1(\Sigma_g, \Z)$).  The sharp dichotomy:
\begin{itemize}
\item Away from $u = 0$ ($\eta^2$ invertible):
  $\mathfrak G_g[\eta^{-2}]
  \cong \operatorname{Mat}_{2^g}(B_\Theta[\eta^{-2}])$.
  Higher genus is \emph{Morita-invisible}: the module category
  at genus~$g$ is equivalent to genus~$0$.
\item On the strict locus $u = 0$:
  $\mathfrak G_g / (\eta^2) \cong B/(\Theta) \otimes
  \Lambda^{2g+1}$.  The genus structure degenerates to an
  exterior-algebra extension by $H_1(\Sigma_g)$.
\end{itemize}
The anomaly-completed system is the completed MC element
$\hat\alpha_T \in \mc(\hat\gSC_T)$, where the completion is by
the strong filtration axiom of Vol~I.  The full algebraic
development is in
Chapter~\ref{app:anomaly-completed-topological-holography}.

\subsection*{From the half-space to the doubling principle}

The affine theorem rests on an accident: linear Poisson structures
localise the Kontsevich integral on wheel graphs, killing all loops
beyond one.  For nonlinear Poisson vertex algebras---Virasoro,
$\mathcal{W}_3$, and beyond---the half-space problem requires all
loop orders.

The resolution is the \emph{doubling theorem}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:doubling-rwi}).  Embed the half-space
$X = \C \times \R_{\ge 0}$ into its double $X_{\mathrm{dbl}} = \C
\times \R$ via the method of images:
$(z,t) \mapsto \bigl((z,t),\, (\bar z, -t)\bigr)$.  The reflected
propagator on the half-space is the pullback of the standard
propagator on the double:
\[
K^{\mathrm{refl}}(p_i, p_j)
\;=\;
K(p_i, p_j) \;+\; K(p_i, \sigma(p_j)),
\qquad
\sigma(z,t) = (\bar z, -t).
\]
This is the method of images, written operadically.  The
reflection $\sigma$ interchanges the two copies; the Neumann
boundary condition is the invariance under $\sigma$.

The four types of codimension-$1$ boundary strata on the
compactified half-space---bulk clusters, boundary clusters, mixed
clusters, and reflection strata---are the $\sigma$-orbits of
the standard FM boundary strata on the double.  The reflected
weight identity (the condition for the boundary algebra to be a
vertex algebra) is the $\sigma$-invariant restriction of the
standard whole-space Stokes identity:
\[
0 \;=\; \int_{\partial\overline{\Conf}_{2n}(X_{\mathrm{dbl}})}
\operatorname{Res}\Bigl(\bigwedge_{e} \omega_e\Bigr)
\quad\xrightarrow{\;\sigma\text{-restrict}\;}\quad
0 \;=\; \sum_S \operatorname{Res}_S\Bigl(
\bigwedge_{e} \omega_e^{\mathrm{refl}}\Bigr).
\]
The half-space does not introduce new analytic content.  It
reorganises the whole-space Kontsevich machinery through $\sigma$.

The chain of implication is complete:
\[
\text{logarithmic $\SCchtop$-algebra}
\;\xRightarrow{\;\text{double}\;}
\text{reflected weight identity}
\;\xRightarrow{\;\text{operadic Stokes}\;}
\text{boundary vertex algebra}.
\]
Every logarithmic $\SCchtop$-algebra has a boundary vertex algebra
(Theorem~\ref{thm:general-half-space-bv}).  The affine case is the
special case where the doubling localises on wheels.  The affine
Poisson--vertex sigma model satisfies the hypotheses of the
physics bridge theorem---BV data, one-loop finiteness, polynomial
interactions---and is therefore a logarithmic $\SCchtop$-algebra
(Proposition~\ref{prop:affine-is-log-SC}).  Drinfeld--Sokolov
reduction preserves the $\SCchtop$ structure
(Proposition~\ref{prop:ds-preserves-SC}), so the Virasoro and
$\mathcal{W}_N$ algebras inherit it
(Corollary~\ref{cor:virasoro-wn-are-SC}).  The entire
standard landscape enters the framework through these two gates:
the affine bridge and DS transport.

D \;=\; \sum_{g \ge 0} \hbar^g D^{(g)},
\qquad
D^{(g)} = \sum_{\substack{\Gamma \in \StGraph^{\mathrm{conn}}
\\ g(\Gamma) = g}} \frac{1}{|\Aut(\Gamma)|}\, D_\Gamma,
\]
is the Feynman transform of the modular operad
$\{\overline{\cM}_{g,n}\}$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:formal-genus-expansion}).  The identity $D^2 = 0$
decomposes into genus-by-genus identities
$\sum_{p+q=g} D^{(p)} D^{(q)} = 0$: the tree-level differential
squares to zero, the one-loop correction is a derivation, and
the higher-genus terms satisfy the modular sewing relations.  The
canonical Maurer--Cartan element
$\delta_\cA = D - D^{(0)} \in F^1\Coder(\barB^{\mathrm{full}}_X(\cA))$
is the total positive-genus correction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:coderivation-MC-synthesis}).  Its genus-$g$
component $\delta_\cA^{(g)}$ is computed by integration over
stable graphs of genus~$g$, with the symmetry factor
$1/|\Aut(\Gamma)|$ that is the mathematical shadow of the
't~Hooft $1/N$ counting.  The combinatorial structure---stable
graphs, automorphism weights, genus bookkeeping---is identical
to the 't~Hooft expansion.  The identification of $\hbar$ with
$1/N$ for a specific holographic model remains the principal
open problem connecting this algebraic machine to gauge-theory
holography (Conjecture~\ref{conj:thooft-gl-n}).

\subsection*{The relative holographic deformation complex}

The correct controller of the holographic lifting problem
is not the 3d Swiss-cheese algebra alone, but its
\emph{relative fibre} over the closed modular datum.
Let $p\colon\gSC_T\to\gAmod$ be the forgetful
$L_\infty$-morphism.  Fix
$\alpha_T\in\mc(\gSC_T)$ with
$p(\alpha_T)=\Theta_\cA$.  Then
$\mathfrak{h}_T^\Theta:=\operatorname{fib}(p)_{\alpha_T}$
controls all lifts: its MC elements are full $3$d data
compatible with the fixed boundary datum, and the
graded obstruction tower
$[\mathfrak{o}_r(\beta)]
\in H^2(\operatorname{gr}^F_r\mathfrak{h}_T^\Theta)$
measures the order-by-order cost of extension
(line-side deformations at stage~$1$, bulk/boundary
comparison at mixed stages, nonlinear/genus
compatibility at higher stages).  The \emph{relative
quartic obstruction}
$\mathfrak{R}_4^{\mathrm{rel}}(T)\in
H^2(\operatorname{gr}^F_4\mathfrak{h}_T^\Theta)$
is the first invariant detecting when a boundary
datum cannot arise from a planar holographic
realisation.

\subsection*{Cyclic trace and the literal 't~Hooft
expansion}

A bare modular graph sum gives a genus-organised
expansion but not a literal 't~Hooft sum: bare stable
graphs do not determine faces, hence carry no canonical
$N$-weight.  Three additional structures close the gap
(Vol~I, Chapter~\ref{chap:e1-modular-koszul}):
a \emph{cyclic trace}~$\tau$ on the open $E_1$-colour
turns each vertex into a cyclic tensor, hence into a
ribbon vertex (Vol~I, Theorem~\ref{thm:cyclicity-ribbon});
a \emph{matrix realisation}
$O_N=\operatorname{Mat}_N\otimes O_0$ with
$\tau_N=\operatorname{Tr}_N\otimes\tau_0$ gives every
face a factor of~$N$; and the resulting amplitude is
$N^{V-E+F}=N^{\chi(\Sigma_\Gamma)}$, the literal
't~Hooft weight
(Vol~I, Theorem~\ref{thm:exact-n-chi-weighting}).
The correct holographic language is therefore
\emph{ribbonised modular Swiss-cheese theory}
$\mathrm{RSC}_{\mathrm{tr}}^{\mathrm{mod}}$.
The \emph{Loop--Connes principle}
$\beta_{\mathrm{der}}^*(\Delta_{\mathrm{open}})
=\Lambda_P$
(Vol~I, Conjecture~\ref{conj:loop-connes-transfer}):
closed genus creation is the image of the open
BV operator under the derived-centre map.

\subsection*{The arithmetic face (Vol~I)}

Volume~I's arithmetic programme reveals that the
shadow tower carries both algebraic and arithmetic
content.  The \emph{arithmetic packet connection}
$\nabla_\cA^{\mathrm{arith}}=d-\bigoplus_\chi
d\log\Lambda_\chi(s)\cdot(\mathrm{id}+N_\chi)\,ds$
is flat, with singular divisor determined by the
\emph{arithmetic skeleton}
$\mathrm{Ask}(\cA)=M_\cA^{\mathrm{ss}}$
and unipotent monodromy from the \emph{algebraic
defect module}
$\mathrm{Def}_{\mathrm{alg}}(\cA)=\bigoplus_\chi
N_\chi M_\chi$.  The principle:
\emph{arithmetic is semisimple; homotopy defect is
unipotent.}  For principal~$\cW_N$, the
\emph{finite Miura defect theorem}
(Vol~I, Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-miura-defect})
decomposes the vacuum character into an $(N{-}1)$-fold
Heisenberg core and a finite polynomial correction;
the Heisenberg core is arithmetically inert
(Vol~I, Proposition~\ref{prop:miura-packet-splitting}).
Three closure conjectures form a strict hierarchy:
quartic closure $\Rightarrow$ Beilinson functional
$\Rightarrow$ clutching closure, with the last using
the full nonlinear modular boundary geometry.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Holographic reconstruction.}\enspace
The chiral algebra fixes a formal local bulk model.  The Hochschild--Kostant--Rosenberg theorem for
Lagrangian embeddings gives $\HH^\bullet(\cA) \simeq \cO(T^*[-1]\cL_\cA)$, and the
shifted cotangent $T^*[-1]\cL_\cA$ is the underlying local model on which the
formal Darboux theorem builds a $(-2)$-shifted ambient once a compatible closed
$1$-form is supplied (Theorem~\ref{thm:holographic-reconstruction}).  The boundary
therefore determines the shifted-cotangent side of the bulk, while the full formal
ambient still requires the Darboux datum that identifies the actual bulk geometry.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The completeness question.}\enspace
The holographic Lagrangian $\cL_\cA^{(0)}$ is constructed from genus-$0$ data: the bar-cobar adjunction on $\FM_k(\C) \times \Conf_k^<(\R)$.  \emph{Is the holographic Lagrangian complete?}  That is, does $\cL_\cA^{(0)}$ extend to a family $\cL_\cA^{(g)} \hookrightarrow \cM_{\mathrm{vac}}$ over the moduli $\overline{\cM}_{g,n}$, compatible with gluing along boundary strata?  For algebras with clean self-intersection (classes~$\mathbf{G}$ and~$\mathbf{L}$: Heisenberg, Kac--Moody), the Lagrangian meets itself transversally and the scalar curvature $d^2 = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_g$ extends automatically.  For algebras with excess self-intersection (class~$\mathbf{M}$: Virasoro, $\mathcal{W}_N$), the derived self-intersection carries higher Tor, the Swiss-cheese operations $m_k$ are successive obstruction classes, and the question is open.  (Classes~$\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{L}$, $\mathbf{C}$ are chirally Koszul---the PBW spectral sequence concentrates and bar cohomology is concentrated.  Class~$\mathbf{M}$ has infinite shadow depth and non-formal Swiss-cheese structure: the fourth-order pole forces nonvanishing transferred operations $m_k^{\mathrm{SC}} \neq 0$ for all $k \geq 3$, though the algebra is chirally Koszul (PBW spectral sequence concentrates) and well-defined Koszul duals exist.  The difficulty for class~$\mathbf{M}$ is the non-formality: beyond the global self-intersection geometry, the big Koszul dual carries higher $\Ainf$ operations.)  This is the central problem of the modular programme: existence, compatibility, and computation of the higher-genus Lagrangian, developed in Part~V.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The monograph computes the local geometry at a point.}\enspace
A chiral algebra is a point in the derived symplectic category: a Lagrangian
$\cL_\cA \hookrightarrow \cM_{\mathrm{vac}}(\cA)$.  The bar complex is the
endomorphism algebra of this point.  The Swiss-cheese operad is the composition law.
Koszul duality is the symplectic complement.  The genus tower is the deformation of the point
over the moduli of curves.  The holographic principle is the theorem that a point
determines its ambient.  The entire content of both volumes is the local geometry of
the derived symplectic category at this single point.
