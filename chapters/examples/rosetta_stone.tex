\section{The Heisenberg Rosetta Stone}
\label{sec:rosetta-stone}
\index{Rosetta Stone|textbf}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!Rosetta Stone|textbf}
\index{Swiss-cheese operad!Heisenberg example|textbf}

The bar complex $\barB(\cH_k)$ is a Swiss-cheese coalgebra
(Theorem~\ref{thm:bar-swiss-cheese}): the bar differential
$d_{\barB}$ computes OPE residues on $\FM_k(\C)$, and the
deconcatenation coproduct $\Delta$ acts on $\Conf_k^{<}(\R)$.
The Koszul dual $\cA^! = Y(\mathrm{u}(1))$ is the Heisenberg
Yangian (Theorem~\ref{thm:Koszul_dual_Yangian}), and the
duality involution closes: $(\cA^!)^! \simeq \cA$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:duality-involution}).

The Lagrangian self-intersection
$\mathfrak{S}_b = L \times_X L$ is one-dimensional with
transversal crossing.  Koszulness ($m_{k \ge 3} = 0$) is the
statement that this intersection is clean:
$H_*^{BM}(\mathfrak{S}_b)$ is one-dimensional in each stratum.
The single scalar~$k$ determines the full holographic dictionary---algebra, Koszul dual, line category, $R$-matrix, genus tower,
PVA---because the Lagrangian is concentrated.

The present section proves this for the Heisenberg algebra
$\cH_k$ by explicit computation.  The results are neither deep
nor difficult; they are \emph{explicit}, and the explicitness
carries information that no amount of abstract machinery can supply.
The Heisenberg algebra has a single generator~$J$, a single
OPE pole of order~$2$, and no composite fields.  These three
simplifications---one generator, one pole order, no composites---make
every step checkable by hand.  Yet none of the five main theorems
of Volume~I becomes trivial: bar-cobar adjunction (Theorem~A),
inversion (Theorem~B), complementarity (Theorem~C), the modular
characteristic (Theorem~D), and Hochschild cohomology (Theorem~H)
are all instantiated here with full content.

The section has five parts:
\S\ref{subsec:rosetta-swiss-cheese} identifies the bar complex
as a Swiss-cheese coalgebra;
\S\ref{subsec:rosetta-operations} computes all higher operations
and verifies that they vanish;
\S\ref{subsec:rosetta-pva} extracts the Poisson vertex algebra
on cohomology; \S\ref{subsec:rosetta-genus1} exhibits
curvature at genus~$1$; and \S\ref{subsec:rosetta-bridge}
assembles a dictionary mapping every bar-complex datum to both a
Volume~I theorem and a Volume~II physical identification.

\subsection{The bar complex as a Swiss-cheese algebra}
\label{subsec:rosetta-swiss-cheese}
\index{bar complex!Swiss-cheese structure!Heisenberg}
% ================================================================

We fix notation.  Let $V = \bC \cdot J$ be the one-dimensional
vector space spanned by the Heisenberg current, let $k \in \bC$
be the level, and let $\cH_k$ be the corresponding Heisenberg
chiral algebra with OPE
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-heisenberg-ope}
J(z)\, J(w) \;\sim\; \frac{k}{(z - w)^2}.
\end{equation}
The bar complex $\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k)$ is the cofree
conilpotent coalgebra on the desuspension $s^{-1}\overline{\cH}_k$,
equipped with the bar differential $d_{\barB}$ constructed from
OPE residues on the Fulton--MacPherson compactification.
Concretely, a bar element of tensor degree~$n$ lives on
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-bar-element-space}
\barB^n(\cH_k) \;=\;
(s^{-1}\overline{\cH}_k)^{\otimes n}
\;\otimes\;
\Omega^{\bullet}\bigl(\overline{\Conf}_{n+1}(X)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\overline{\Conf}_{n+1}(X) = \FM_{n+1}(X)$ is the
Fulton--MacPherson compactification and the logarithmic forms
$\eta_{ij} = d\log(z_i - z_j)$ provide the propagators.

\begin{theorem}[Heisenberg bar complex is a Swiss-cheese algebra;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:rosetta-swiss-cheese}
\index{Swiss-cheese operad!Heisenberg bar complex}
The geometric bar complex $\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k)$ with:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item bar differential $d_{\barB}$
  \textup{(}residues on collision divisors of\/ $\FM_n(\bC)$\textup{)}
  as closed-color structure,
\item deconcatenation coproduct $\Delta$
  \textup{(}ordered splitting\textup{)} as open-color structure,
\end{enumerate}
is an algebra over $\SCchtop$, verifying the Steinberg
presentation \textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:steinberg-presentation})}
for the simplest nontrivial chiral algebra.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof has four steps, each corresponding to one axiom of the
Swiss-cheese operad.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step~1: Closed color.}\enspace
The bar differential $d_{\barB}$ is the coderivation of the
cofree coalgebra $T^c(s^{-1}\overline{\cH}_k)$ determined by
the OPE residue.  For the Heisenberg algebra, the only nonvanishing
component is the binary one:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-dbar-binary}
d_{\barB}\bigl[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J\bigr]
\;=\;
\int_{E_{12}}
  J(z_1)\, J(z_2) \cdot \omega_{12}^{\FM}
\;=\;
J_{(1)}J
\;=\; k.
\end{equation}
The bar differential is the Stokes boundary operator on $\FM_2(\bC)$:
integration over the exceptional divisor $E_{12} \cong S^1$ in
the FM blowup extracts the mode product $J_{(1)}J = k$ from
the double pole $k/(z_1 - z_2)^2$.

The nilpotence $d_{\barB}^2 = 0$ is the Arnold relation
on $\FM_3(\bC)$.  The three codimension-$1$ boundary strata of
$\FM_3(\bC)$ are the collision divisors $\colldiv{1}{2}$,
$\colldiv{2}{3}$, and $\colldiv{1}{3}$.
On these strata, the logarithmic $2$-form
$\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23}$ restricts to the Arnold relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-arnold}
[\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23}]
\;+\;
[\eta_{23} \wedge \eta_{13}]
\;+\;
[\eta_{13} \wedge \eta_{12}]
\;=\; 0
\end{equation}
in $H^2(\FM_3(\bC))$.
Each summand contributes a residue to $d_{\barB}^2$, and their
sum vanishes by exactness.  For the Heisenberg algebra, this
computation produces $d_{\barB}^2[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
= k \cdot (\text{Arnold sum}) = 0$.

The closed-color structure is thus a dg coalgebra
$(\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k), d_{\barB})$, with $d_{\barB}$
built from $\FM_n(\bC)$.  This is holomorphic factorization.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step~2: Open color.}\enspace
The deconcatenation coproduct
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-delta}
\Delta\bigl[a_1 \,|\, \cdots \,|\, a_n\bigr]
\;=\;
\sum_{i=0}^{n}
\bigl[a_1 \,|\, \cdots \,|\, a_i\bigr]
\;\otimes\;
\bigl[a_{i+1} \,|\, \cdots \,|\, a_n\bigr]
\end{equation}
is coassociative by construction: it is the tensor coalgebra
coproduct.  The ordering of tensor factors corresponds to the
ordering of points $t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_n$ on $\bR$;
splitting at position~$i$ is cutting the interval
$[t_1, t_n] \subset \bR$ at the gap between $t_i$ and $t_{i+1}$.
The ordered configuration space $\Conf_n^{<}(\bR)$ is
contractible (it is an open simplex
$\{t_1 < \cdots < t_n\} \cong \bR^{n-1}_{> 0}$), so the
$\Eone$-structure on $\Conf_n(\bR)$ contracts to
$\Ass$-structure: $\Delta$ is strictly coassociative,
not merely $\Eone$-coassociative up to homotopy.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step~3: Compatibility.}\enspace
The coderivation property
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-compatibility}
\Delta \circ d_{\barB}
\;=\;
(\id \otimes d_{\barB} \;+\; d_{\barB} \otimes \id)
\circ \Delta
\end{equation}
asserts that $d_{\barB}$ is a coderivation of $\Delta$.
We verify this on bar elements of degree~$2$.  The left-hand side
gives
\[
\Delta\bigl(d_{\barB}[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]\bigr)
\;=\;
\Delta(k)
\;=\;
k \otimes [] + [] \otimes k,
\]
where $[]$ denotes the empty bar element (the coaugmentation).
The right-hand side gives
\[
(d_{\barB} \otimes \id + \id \otimes d_{\barB})\bigl(
  [] \otimes [s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
  \;+\;
  [s^{-1}J] \otimes [s^{-1}J]
  \;+\;
  [s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J] \otimes []
\bigr).
\]
The middle term $[s^{-1}J] \otimes [s^{-1}J]$ contributes nothing
because $d_{\barB}$ on a single element $[s^{-1}J]$ requires a
binary collision, which is absent.  The first and last terms
contribute $[] \otimes k + k \otimes []$, matching the left-hand side.

Geometrically,
the collision divisors $\colldiv{i}{j} \subset \FM_n(\bC)$
parametrize collisions in the holomorphic direction, while the
splitting $\Delta$ acts in the topological direction on $\bR$.
These directions are independent in the product
$\FM_n(\bC) \times \Conf_n^{<}(\bR)$: a collision
$z_i \to z_j$ in $\bC$ does not merge the corresponding
points $t_i, t_j \in \bR$.  The residue operation (extracting the
singular part of the OPE along $\colldiv{i}{j}$) therefore
commutes with the splitting operation (cutting the interval at a
gap in the $\bR$-ordering).

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step~4: Coherences.}\enspace
The Boardman--Vogt $W$-construction
$W(\SCchtop)$ replaces strict compatibility with a family of
homotopy-coherent conditions.  For $(\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k),
d_{\barB}, \Delta)$, all coherence conditions are automatically
satisfied at the strict level:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item the closed-color differential $d_{\barB}$ is strictly
  square-zero (Arnold relation, not merely $\Ainf$-null);
\item the open-color coproduct $\Delta$ is strictly coassociative
  (tensor coalgebra, not merely $\Eone$-coassociative);
\item compatibility~\eqref{eq:rosetta-compatibility} holds strictly,
  not up to a homotopy parametrized by the $W$-construction.
\end{enumerate}
The $W$-construction coherences between $\FM_n(\bC)$ and
$\Conf_n^{<}(\bR)$ are trivial because the product
$\FM_n(\bC) \times \Conf_n^{<}(\bR)$ decomposes: the FM operad is
formal at genus~$0$ (Kontsevich), so all higher coherences on the
holomorphic factor vanish; the ordered configuration space on $\bR$
is contractible, so all higher coherences on the topological factor
vanish; and the product structure admits no mixed higher coherences.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Reading off the higher operations}
\label{subsec:rosetta-operations}
\index{higher operations!from bar complex}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!higher operations}
% ================================================================

The Swiss-cheese structure of the bar complex carries all the
information of an $\Ainf$ chiral algebra.  The following
proposition makes the extraction explicit.

\begin{proposition}[Higher operations from bar elements;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:rosetta-operations}
\index{A-infinity operations@$\Ainf$ operations!from bar complex}
The $\Ainf$ operations $m_k$ of a chiral algebra $\cA$ are
recovered from the bar complex by the formula
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-mk-extraction}
m_k(a_1, \ldots, a_k;\, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_{k-1})
\;=\;
\text{coefficient of }
s^{-1}a_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes s^{-1}a_k
\text{ in }
d_{\barB}^{(k)},
\end{equation}
where $d_{\barB}^{(k)}$ denotes the component of the bar
differential that maps $\barB^k \to \barB^{k-1}$ and the
spectral parameters $\lambda_j$ arise from the regularization
of the collision integral on $\FM_k(\bC) \times \Conf_k^{<}(\bR)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is the defining property of the bar construction as a
coderivation: $d_{\barB}$ is the unique coderivation of
$T^c(s^{-1}\overline{\cA})$ whose composition with the
projection $T^c(s^{-1}\overline{\cA}) \twoheadrightarrow
s^{-1}\overline{\cA}$ recovers the sequence of operations
$(m_1, m_2, m_3, \ldots)$.  The spectral parameter
$\lambda_j$ arises from the regularized integral
$\int_{\Conf_k^{<}(\bR)} dt_1 \cdots dt_{k-1}$
along the topological direction, with $\lambda_j = t_{j+1} - t_j$
recording the separation between consecutive points.
\end{proof}

We now compute all $m_k$ for $\cH_k$.

\begin{proposition}[Heisenberg higher operations;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!$m_k$ operations}
For the Heisenberg algebra $\cH_k$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item $m_1 = 0$ \textup{(}no internal differential\textup{)}.
\item $m_2(J, J;\, \lambda) = k\lambda$
  \textup{(}the OPE residue\textup{)}.
\item $m_k = 0$ for all $k \geq 3$
  \textup{(}no ternary or higher operations\textup{)}.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, $\cH_k$ is \emph{formal}: its $\Ainf$ structure is
concentrated in $m_2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(i) The internal differential of $\cH_k$ is zero: there is no
$Q$-operator, and the algebra has trivial cohomological grading
concentrated in degree~$0$.

\smallskip
(ii) The binary operation extracts the double-pole mode.  From
equation~\eqref{eq:rosetta-dbar-binary}, the bar differential
on a degree-$2$ element is
\[
d_{\barB}[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J]
\;=\;
J_{(1)}J
\;=\; k.
\]
In the spectral (Fourier) parametrization, the meromorphic function
$k/(z_1 - z_2)^2$ corresponds to the spectral expression
$m_2(J, J;\, \lambda) = k\lambda$.  The spectral parameter
$\lambda$ records the weight of the residue: a pole of order~$2$
at $z_1 = z_2$ produces a first-order polynomial in~$\lambda$
via the substitution $(z_1 - z_2)^{-n-1} \leftrightarrow
\lambda^{(n)}/n!$.

\smallskip
(iii) The vanishing of $m_k$ for $k \geq 3$ is the decisive
computation.  Consider the degree-$3$ bar element
\[
\xi_3
\;=\;
s^{-1}J \otimes s^{-1}J \otimes s^{-1}J
\;\otimes\;
\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23}
\;\in\;
\barB^3(\cH_k).
\]
To compute $d_{\barB}^{(3)}$, one must extract a residue
from the iterated OPE of three Heisenberg currents along a
codimension-$2$ stratum of $\FM_3(\bC)$.  The iterated OPE is
\begin{align}\label{eq:rosetta-iterated-ope}
J(z_1)\, J(z_2)\, J(z_3)
&\;\sim\;
\frac{k}{(z_1 - z_2)^2}\, J(z_3)
\;+\;
\frac{k}{(z_1 - z_3)^2}\, J(z_2)
\;+\;
\frac{k}{(z_2 - z_3)^2}\, J(z_1).
\end{align}
Each term has a double pole in one pair of variables and is
regular in the third.  The ternary operation $m_3$ would arise
from a \emph{simultaneous} triple collision $z_1 \to z_2 \to z_3$.
On $\FM_3(\bC)$, the triple collision stratum is a point
(the maximal boundary stratum).  The residue at this point requires
a pole of order $\geq 3$ in the combined variables.  But the
iterated OPE~\eqref{eq:rosetta-iterated-ope} has at most
double poles in each pair --- there is no pole of the required
order in the ternary collision limit.

More precisely: in the coordinates $u = z_1 - z_2$,
$v = z_2 - z_3$ on $\FM_3(\bC)$, the triple collision
$u, v \to 0$ has codimension~$2$.  The form
$\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23} = d\log u \wedge d\log v$
contributes a factor $u^{-1}v^{-1}\, du \wedge dv$.  The
OPE contributes at most $u^{-2}$ or $v^{-2}$ (never both
simultaneously, because there is no term with $u^{-2}v^{-2}$
in~\eqref{eq:rosetta-iterated-ope}).  The combined integrand
is therefore at most $u^{-3}v^{-1}$ or $u^{-1}v^{-3}$ in
the worst case: the residue in the \emph{other} variable
vanishes because the pole order is insufficient.  Hence
$m_3 = 0$.

The same argument applies at all higher degrees: $m_k = 0$
for $k \geq 3$ because the Heisenberg OPE has only a double
pole, and the $k$-fold collision on $\FM_k(\bC)$ requires
poles of total order $\geq k$ to produce a nonvanishing
residue against the logarithmic $k$-form
$\eta_{12} \wedge \eta_{23} \wedge \cdots \wedge \eta_{k-1,k}$.
The double-pole OPE can produce at most order~$2$ in each pairwise
variable, but the factorization of the iterated OPE ensures that
no more than one pair is singular at a time.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Formality and Koszulness]
\label{rem:rosetta-formality-koszulness}
\index{formality!Heisenberg}
\index{Koszulness!Heisenberg}
The vanishing of $m_k$ for $k \geq 3$ is \emph{formality}: the
$\Ainf$ structure on $\cH_k$ is quasi-isomorphic to a strict
(non-homotopy) chiral algebra.  This is a consequence of the
general one-loop criterion (Corollary~\ref{cor:affine-koszul}:
one-loop exact algebras without DS reduction are Koszul), and
is equivalent to the chiral Koszulness of $\cH_k$
(Theorem~\ref*{thm:frame-heisenberg-bar}): the PBW spectral
sequence degenerates at~$E_1$, and there are no higher Massey
products.  For non-abelian algebras (affine Kac--Moody, Virasoro,
$\mathcal{W}$-algebras), the simple-pole OPE terms produce
nonvanishing $m_3$ and the theory ceases to be formal.
The non-formality is detected by the Chevalley--Eilenberg
differential on the bar complex, which is the $d_1$ page of
the PBW spectral sequence.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Lagrangian content of the perturbative/non-perturbative divide]
\label{rem:rosetta-lagrangian-perturbative}
\index{perturbative!Heisenberg as clean intersection}
\index{non-perturbative!Virasoro as excess intersection}
The Heisenberg is perturbatively exact because its Lagrangian
self-intersection is clean: the double-pole OPE produces a
transversal crossing with one-dimensional strata, $m_k = 0$
for $k \ge 3$, and the genus tower is controlled by a single
scalar $\kappa(\cH_k) = k$.  The Virasoro, by contrast, is
non-perturbative: its fourth-order pole $c/(z-w)^4$ creates
excess intersection dimension, forcing nonvanishing higher
$\Ainf$ operations that proliferate like instanton corrections
through the genus tower.  This is not a coincidence---it is the
Lagrangian content of the perturbative/non-perturbative divide
(Remark~\ref{rem:koszul-perturbative-watershed}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Poisson vertex algebra on cohomology}
\label{subsec:rosetta-pva}
\index{Poisson vertex algebra!Heisenberg}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!PVA structure}
% ================================================================

The binary operation $m_2$ decomposes into a regular part (the
commutative product) and a singular part (the $\lambda$-bracket).
For the Heisenberg algebra, this decomposition reveals the
simplest nontrivial Poisson vertex algebra.

\begin{proposition}[Heisenberg PVA; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{prop:rosetta-pva}
\index{lambda-bracket@$\lambda$-bracket!Heisenberg}
The cohomology $H^{\bullet}(\cH_k, Q) = \bC[J]$ carries the
structure of a Poisson vertex algebra with:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item\label{item:rosetta-pva-product}
  commutative product: $J \cdot J = 0$
  \textup{(}the regular part of $m_2$ vanishes for the
  Heisenberg self-OPE\textup{)};
\item\label{item:rosetta-pva-bracket}
  $\lambda$-bracket: $\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$
  \textup{(}the singular part of $m_2$\textup{)}.
\end{enumerate}
This is the free boson PVA: the Poisson vertex algebra of a free
scalar field with propagator $k/(z - w)^2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The binary operation $m_2(J, J;\, \lambda) = k\lambda$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk}(ii)) decomposes
according to the pole order:
\begin{align}
m_2(J, J;\, \lambda)
&\;=\;
\underbrace{0}_{\text{regular part}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{k\lambda}_{\text{singular part}}.
\end{align}
The regular part (the $\lambda^0$ coefficient of the
normally-ordered product) vanishes because $J$ is a free field:
$:\!J(z)\, J(w)\!:\,$ is regular at $z = w$, and no normal-ordering
constant is produced.  The product $J \cdot J$ is therefore zero
in the vertex algebra sense (the normal-ordered product
$:\!JJ\!:\,$ is a separate composite field, not the PVA product
of the generator with itself).

The singular part is the $\lambda$-bracket
$\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$, encoding the double-pole OPE
$J(z)J(w) \sim k/(z-w)^2$.

The PVA axioms are verified as follows.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Sesquilinearity.}\enspace
$\{\partial J_\lambda J\} = -\lambda \{J_\lambda J\} = -k\lambda^2$
and $\{J_\lambda \partial J\} = (\partial + \lambda)\{J_\lambda J\}
= (\partial + \lambda)(k\lambda) = k\lambda^2$
(where $\partial$ acts on the vacuum, giving zero).

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Skew-symmetry.}\enspace
The PVA skew-symmetry axiom reads
$\{a_\lambda b\} = -\{b_{-\lambda - \partial} a\}$.
With $a = b = J$:
$-\{J_{-\lambda - \partial} J\} = -k(-\lambda - \partial) = k\lambda + k\partial$.
Acting on the vacuum ($\partial \cdot \mathbf{1} = 0$), this reduces to $k\lambda = \{J_\lambda J\}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Jacobi identity.}\enspace
With a single generator $J$, the Jacobi identity
\[
\{J_\lambda \{J_\mu J\}\}
\;-\;
\{J_\mu \{J_\lambda J\}\}
\;=\;
\{\{J_\lambda J\}_{\lambda + \mu} J\}
\]
reduces to $\{J_\lambda\, k\mu\} - \{J_\mu\, k\lambda\} = \{(k\lambda)_{\lambda+\mu}\, J\}$.
Since $k\mu$ and $k\lambda$ are scalars (not elements of the algebra), all three brackets vanish.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Leibniz rule.}\enspace
$\{J_\lambda (J \cdot J)\} = \{J_\lambda 0\} = 0$, and
$J \cdot \{J_\lambda J\} + \{J_\lambda J\} \cdot J
= J \cdot (k\lambda) + (k\lambda) \cdot J = 0$
(since $J \cdot c = 0$ for a scalar $c$ in the PVA with zero
product).  Both sides are zero: Leibniz is satisfied.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Connection to the coisson structure]
\label{rem:rosetta-coisson}
\index{coisson algebra!Heisenberg}
The PVA of Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-pva} is the classical
limit of the chiral algebra $\cH_k$: it is the Beilinson--Drinfeld
coisson structure on the commutative $\mathcal{D}_X$-algebra
underlying $\cH_k$.  The $\lambda$-bracket $\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$
encodes the symplectic pairing on $V = \bC \cdot J$: the coefficient
$k$ of $\lambda$ is the level.  This is the simplest
instance of the general principle that the Koszul dual
$\cA^!$ of a chiral algebra $\cA$ is controlled by the
$\lambda$-bracket of its coisson structure
(Definition~\ref*{def:coisson}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Genus 1: curvature appears}
\label{subsec:rosetta-genus1}
\index{curvature!genus 1!Heisenberg}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!genus 1 curvature}
\index{Swiss-cheese operad!curved}
% ================================================================

At genus~$0$, the bar complex $(\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k),
d_{\barB})$ is an honest chain complex: $d_{\barB}^2 = 0$,
and the Swiss-cheese structure is flat.  At genus~$1$, the
situation changes qualitatively.  The propagator on an elliptic
curve $E_\tau = \bC / (\Z + \Z\tau)$ is no longer single-valued:
the Weierstrass zeta function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-elliptic-propagator}
G_\tau(z)
\;=\;
\zeta_\tau(z) \;+\; \frac{\pi^2}{3}\, E_2(\tau) \cdot z
\end{equation}
has $B$-cycle monodromy $G_\tau(z + \tau) - G_\tau(z) = -2\pi i$.
This monodromy, absent at genus~$0$, is the source of curvature.

\begin{theorem}[Curved Swiss-cheese at genus~$1$;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:rosetta-curved}
\index{curved Swiss-cheese!genus 1}
At genus $g = 1$, the bar complex $\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k)$ with:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item fiberwise differential $\dfib$ \textup{(}residues using
  the elliptic propagator $d\log E_\tau(z, w)$\textup{)},
\item coproduct $\Delta$ \textup{(}unchanged from genus~$0$\textup{)},
\end{enumerate}
satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
\item\label{item:rosetta-curved-delta}
  $\Delta$ is coassociative \textup{(}same proof as genus~$0$\textup{)};
\item\label{item:rosetta-curved-coder}
  $\dfib$ is a coderivation of $\Delta$;
\item\label{item:rosetta-curved-dsquared}
  $\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1$, where
  $\omega_1 = c_1(\mathbb{E}) \in H^2(\overline{\cM}_1)$
  is the first Chern class of the Hodge bundle;
\item\label{item:rosetta-curved-corrected}
  the total corrected differential
  $\Dg{1} = \dfib + \text{\textup{period correction}}$
  satisfies $\Dg{1}^{\,2} = 0$.
\end{enumerate}
The structure $(\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k), \dfib, \Delta)$ is a
\emph{curved} $\SCchtop$-coalgebra: the closed-color
differential is curved, the open-color coproduct is flat,
and the curvature is invisible to the topological direction.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\ref{item:rosetta-curved-delta}:
The coproduct $\Delta$ is the tensor coalgebra structure,
defined by the ordering of tensor factors.  It depends only on the
combinatorial data of the bar complex, not on the propagator
or the genus of the curve.  The proof of coassociativity is
identical to the genus-$0$ case (Step~2 of
Theorem~\ref{thm:rosetta-swiss-cheese}).

\medskip
\ref{item:rosetta-curved-coder}:
The fiberwise differential $\dfib$ on $E_\tau$ is constructed
by the same residue procedure as $d_{\barB}$ at genus~$0$,
replacing the rational propagator $d\log(z - w)$ with the
elliptic propagator $dG_\tau(z - w)$.  On a fixed elliptic
curve $E_\tau$, the binary component is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-dfib-binary}
\dfib\bigl[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J\bigr]
\;=\;
\Res_{z_1 = z_2}\!\left[
  \frac{k}{(z_1 - z_2)^2}
  \cdot dG_\tau(z_1 - z_2)
\right].
\end{equation}
The coderivation property $\Delta \circ \dfib
= (\id \otimes \dfib + \dfib \otimes \id) \circ \Delta$
holds for the same geometric reason as at genus~$0$: the
residue operation on $E_\tau$ acts on the holomorphic
coordinates $z_i$, while $\Delta$ acts on the tensor ordering
(the topological coordinates $t_i$).  These directions remain
independent at genus~$1$.

\medskip
\ref{item:rosetta-curved-dsquared}:
We compute $\dfib^{\,2}$.
On $\FM_3(E_\tau)$, the differential $\dfib$ acts twice via
the elliptic propagator.  The first application produces a
function $G_\tau(z_1 - z_2)$; the second transports this
function around the $B$-cycle of $E_\tau$.  The
$B$-cycle monodromy of $G_\tau$ contributes a constant shift:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-monodromy}
G_\tau(z + \tau) - G_\tau(z) = -2\pi i.
\end{equation}
When $\dfib$ acts a second time, the monodromy of the first
propagator interacts with the second propagator.  The
double-pole OPE $J(z_1)\, J(z_2) \sim k/(z_1 - z_2)^2$
contributes a factor of~$k$.  The combined effect is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-dsquared-computation}
\dfib^{\,2}\bigl[s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J \,|\, s^{-1}J\bigr]
\;=\;
k \cdot (-2\pi i)^2 \cdot
\left(
  \text{period integral over } E_\tau
\right)
\;=\;
k \cdot \omega_1,
\end{equation}
where the period integral, after pushforward to
$\overline{\cM}_1$, produces the Hodge class
$\omega_1 = c_1(\mathbb{E})$.

This is the fiberwise curvature.  It is \emph{scalar}: the
coefficient~$k$ is a number, not an operator.  The curvature
is therefore a scalar coderivation of $\Delta$: it commutes
with the coproduct.  The Swiss-cheese structure persists, but
the closed color is curved.

\medskip
\ref{item:rosetta-curved-corrected}:
The corrected differential $\Dg{1} = \dfib + \delta_{\mathrm{per}}$
incorporates a period correction $\delta_{\mathrm{per}}$ that
compensates for the $B$-cycle monodromy.  The period correction
is determined by the requirement $\Dg{1}^{\,2} = 0$: it must
satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-period-correction}
\delta_{\mathrm{per}}^{\,2}
\;+\;
\dfib \circ \delta_{\mathrm{per}}
\;+\;
\delta_{\mathrm{per}} \circ \dfib
\;=\;
-k \cdot \omega_1.
\end{equation}
The existence and uniqueness of $\delta_{\mathrm{per}}$ is the
content of Theorem~\ref*{thm:quantum-diff-squares-zero},
specialized to the Heisenberg algebra.  The period correction
couples the topological direction ($\bR$-ordering) to the Hodge
bundle $\mathbb{E}$ over $\overline{\cM}_1$: it is the
first place where the two colors of the Swiss-cheese
interact nontrivially.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Physical meaning of curvature]
\label{rem:rosetta-curvature-physics}
\index{conformal anomaly!Heisenberg}
The curvature $\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1$ is the conformal
anomaly of the Heisenberg theory.  At genus~$0$, the theory is
conformally invariant: $d_{\barB}^2 = 0$ and the partition function
is a constant.  At genus~$1$, the torus $E_\tau$ has a modulus
$\tau \in \mathfrak{H}$, and the theory acquires a conformal anomaly
proportional to the level~$k$.

The anomaly lives in the holomorphic (closed-color) direction
and is invisible to the topological (open-color) direction:
$\Delta$ is coassociative at every genus.  This is the
Swiss-cheese incarnation of the principle that the conformal
anomaly is a \emph{holomorphic} obstruction.  The central
charge $c = 1$ of the Heisenberg algebra (one free boson)
enters through the modular characteristic $\kappa(\cH_k) = k$
(Theorem~\ref*{thm:modular-characteristic}): the curvature
coefficient is the level, and the level is the basic scalar
invariant of the Koszul pair $(\cH_k, \cH_k^!)$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Genus~$0$ as the flat limit]
\label{rem:rosetta-flat-limit}
The genus-$0$ theory is the \emph{flat} limit of the genus-$1$
theory: as $\tau \to i\infty$ (the cusp of the modular curve),
the elliptic curve $E_\tau$ degenerates to a nodal rational curve,
the propagator $G_\tau(z) \to (z - w)^{-1}$ loses its monodromy,
and the curvature $\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1 \to 0$.  The
Swiss-cheese structure specializes from curved to flat.  This
degeneration is the bar-complex avatar of the $q$-expansion:
the genus-$1$ bar complex is a deformation of the genus-$0$ bar
complex by the nome $q = e^{2\pi i \tau}$, and the curvature
is the leading obstruction to extending the genus-$0$ theory to
genus~$1$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Complementarity for the Heisenberg algebra;
\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{prop:rosetta-complementarity}
\index{complementarity!Heisenberg}
The Heisenberg algebra and its Koszul dual
$\cH_k^! = \mathrm{Sym}^{\mathrm{ch}}(V^*)$
\textup{(}Theorem~\ref*{thm:frame-heisenberg-koszul-dual}\textup{)}
satisfy:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item $\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) = 0$:
  the modular characteristics are opposite;
\item $\mathbf{Q}_1(\cH_k) \oplus \mathbf{Q}_1(\cH_k^!)
  = H^{\bullet}(\overline{\cM}_1,\, \mathcal{Z}_{\cH_k})$:
  the quantum corrections are complementary Lagrangians.
\end{enumerate}
This is Theorem~C
\textup{(Theorem~\ref*{thm:quantum-complementarity-main})}
specialized to the Heisenberg case.  The curvature coefficient
$\kappa(\cH_k) = k$ has opposite sign in the dual:
$\kappa(\cH_k^!) = -k$, reflecting the opposite $B$-cycle
monodromy of the dual propagator.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[The modular characteristic is the level]
\label{rem:rosetta-kappa-is-k}
\index{modular characteristic!Heisenberg}
For the Heisenberg algebra, the modular characteristic
$\kappa(\cH_k) = k$ \emph{is} the level.  This is the simplest
instance of the general relation: $\kappa$ measures the scalar
component of the genus-$1$ curvature
(Theorem~\ref*{thm:modular-characteristic}), and for a single
free boson with propagator $k/(z - w)^2$, the curvature is
$k \cdot \omega_1$ with no further structure.  The $\hat{A}$-genus
generating function for the Heisenberg genus tower is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-ahat}
\hat{A}_{\cH_k}(q)
\;=\;
\prod_{n=1}^{\infty}\,
\frac{n\, q^{n/2}}{e^{n k q^n / 2} - e^{-n k q^n / 2}}
\end{equation}
which, at $k = 1$, specializes to the standard $\hat{A}$-series.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Bar cohomology and Hochschild cohomology;
\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]
\label{prop:rosetta-bar-hochschild}
\index{bar cohomology!Heisenberg}
\index{Hochschild cohomology!Heisenberg}
The bar cohomology of $\cH_k$ at genus~$0$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rosetta-bar-cohomology}
H^n(\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k))
\;=\;
\begin{cases}
\bC & n = 0, \\
0 & n = 1, \\
\bC \cdot c_k & n = 2, \\
0 & n \geq 3,
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
recovering the central extension class $c_k \in H^2$
\textup{(}Theorem~\ref*{thm:frame-heisenberg-bar}\textup{)}.
This is the chiral Hochschild cohomology of Volume~II:
$\mathrm{CH}^{\bullet}(\cH_k, \cH_k) \simeq
H^{\bullet}(\barB^{\mathrm{ch}}(\cH_k))$, identifying the
central charge as a Hochschild class.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[The Rosetta Stone principle]
\label{rem:rosetta-principle}
\index{Rosetta Stone!principle}
The Heisenberg algebra is the simplest chiral algebra, but it is
not trivial: it carries curvature ($\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1$),
complementarity ($\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) = 0$),
the $\hat{A}$-genus, and the full modular structure.
Every feature of the general theory is already present.

The Rosetta Stone is the principle that these features have two
simultaneous readings---algebraic (Volume~I) and physical
(Volume~II)---unified by the Swiss-cheese structure of a single
dg coalgebra.  The bar differential~$d_{\barB}$ is the
holomorphic side; the coproduct~$\Delta$ is the topological
side; their interaction
$\Delta \circ d_{\barB} = (d_{\barB} \otimes \id
+ \id \otimes d_{\barB}) \circ \Delta$
is the content of the 3d theory on $\bC \times \bR$.  At genus~$0$,
both readings are equivalent and either suffices.  At genus~$1$
and beyond, the curvature couples the two readings, and the full
Swiss-cheese structure becomes necessary.

What the Steinberg variety is to the representation theory of
$p$-adic groups---a single geometric object that unifies
Borel--Moore homology with convolution algebras---the Heisenberg
bar complex is to the representation theory of chiral algebras:
a single coalgebra that unifies Koszul duality with holomorphic--topological
factorization.  The flag variety is replaced by the FM
compactification, the nilpotent cone by the collision divisors,
and the convolution product by the deconcatenation coproduct.
The Rosetta Stone is the dictionary that makes this analogy precise.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Heisenberg 3d MC element}
\label{subsec:rosetta-3d-mc}
\index{universal MC element!3d!Heisenberg}
\index{Heisenberg algebra!3d MC element}
% ================================================================

The Swiss-cheese convolution $L_\infty$-algebra
$\gSC_T$ (Definition~\ref{def:sc-convolution}) and its canonical
MC element $\alpha_T$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:3d-universal-mc}) specialise
to the Heisenberg as follows.

\begin{theorem}[Heisenberg 3d MC element; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{thm:rosetta-3d-mc}
For the Heisenberg theory $T = (\cH_k, \cH_{-k}\text{-mod})$ on
$\C \times \R_{\ge 0}$, the Swiss-cheese convolution algebra
$\gSC_T$ is abelian: $[\alpha,\beta] = 0$ for all
$\alpha, \beta \in \gSC_T$.  The MC element is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:alpha-heisenberg}
\alpha_{\cH_k}
\;=\;
\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}
\;+\;
\hbar\,\alpha_{\mathrm{loop}},
\end{equation}
where the two components are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Closed-colour, genus-$0$ component:}
  $\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}$ is the binary operation
  $m_2(J,J;\lambda) = k\lambda$ on $\FM_2(\C)$, encoding the
  double-pole OPE\@.  No higher closed vertices contribute
  ($m_k = 0$ for $k \ge 3$, by
  Proposition~\textup{\ref{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk}}).
\item \emph{Closed-colour, genus-$1$ component:}
  $\alpha_{\mathrm{loop}} = k \cdot \eta \otimes \Lambda$ is the
  genus-$1$ self-loop correction from the unique graph
  $\Gamma_{\mathrm{irr}}$ \textup{(}one vertex of genus~$0$ with
  one self-edge\textup{)}.  The BV operator $\Delta$ traces the
  level $\langle J, J \rangle = k$; the boundary class
  $[\delta_{\mathrm{irr}}]$ gives
  $\omega_1 = \tfrac{1}{12}\delta_{\mathrm{irr}}$.
\end{enumerate}
There is no mixed-colour or open-colour MC contribution: the
abelian structure forces all mixed and open graph sums to vanish.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Abelianity.}\enspace
The Lie bracket on $\gSC_T$ is the operadic composition bracket,
which requires at least one nontrivial vertex operation.  For the
Heisenberg, $m_k = 0$ for $k \ge 3$ and the simple-pole OPE
residue $J_{(0)}J = 0$ vanishes.  Every composition of two graph
contributions involves at least one operation $m_k$ with $k \ge 3$
or a simple-pole insertion, hence vanishes.  The bracket is
identically zero.

\emph{Closed-colour component.}\enspace
The only contributing two-coloured trees with all vertices closed
are those with each vertex labelled by $m_2$ (the binary OPE).
Stability $2g(v) - 2 + \mathrm{val}(v) > 0$ with
$\mathrm{val}(v) = 2$ and $g(v) = 0$ fails ($2 \cdot 0 - 2 + 2 = 0
\not> 0$), so at genus~$0$ no nontrivial tree contributes
beyond the binary operation itself.
The element $\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}$ is the identity
\[
\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}
\;=\;
m_2 \;\in\;
\Hom\bigl(\mathbf{B}^{(0,2)}(\SCop),\;
\End_{\cH_k}\bigr),
\]
encoding the double-pole OPE at the unique two-point closed
vertex.  Its MC equation $D\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}} +
\tfrac{1}{2}[\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}, \alpha_{\mathrm{cl}}] = 0$
reduces to $d_{\barB}^2 = 0$ (the Arnold relation, verified in
Theorem~\ref{thm:rosetta-swiss-cheese}, Step~1).

\emph{Genus-$1$ component.}\enspace
At genus~$1$, the unique graph is $\Gamma_{\mathrm{irr}}$: one
vertex of genus~$0$ with one self-edge and one half-edge.  The
graph contribution is $\hbar \cdot \Delta$, where
$\Delta(sJ) = \langle J, J \rangle \cdot s\mathbf{1} = k \cdot
s\mathbf{1}$.  The chain $W_{\Gamma_{\mathrm{irr}}} =
[\delta_{\mathrm{irr}}]$ gives the Hodge class
$\omega_1 = \tfrac{1}{12}\delta_{\mathrm{irr}}$.  Therefore
$\alpha_{\mathrm{loop}} = k \cdot \eta \otimes \Lambda$, and the
total MC element is $\alpha_{\cH_k} = \alpha_{\mathrm{cl}} +
\hbar\,k \cdot \eta \otimes \Lambda$.

\emph{Mixed and open colours.}\enspace
A mixed-colour graph has at least one open vertex (labelled by an
$E_1$-operation on line modules) and at least one closed vertex.
For the Heisenberg, line modules are modules over
$\cH_{-k}^! = \cH_k$ (the Koszul dual is level inversion;
Theorem~\ref*{thm:heisenberg-koszul-dual-early} of Vol~I).  The
Heisenberg module category is semisimple: every module is a direct
sum of Fock spaces $\mathcal{F}_\mu$, and the $E_1$-operations on
Fock modules are scalar (the vertex operators are abelian).  Every
mixed graph contribution is therefore proportional to a product of
scalars, and the structure maps of $\gSC_T$ annihilate them by the
coderivation property applied to the trivial $m_k = 0$ ($k \ge 3$)
at the closed vertices.  The open-colour contribution vanishes
for the same reason.
\end{proof}

The six projections of Proposition~\ref{prop:alpha-projections}
specialise as follows.

\begin{corollary}[Heisenberg projections; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]
\label{cor:rosetta-heisenberg-projections}
For the Heisenberg theory:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{Closed face} $=$ the boundary $\Ainf$ algebra $\cH_k$
  with $m_2(J,J;\lambda) = k\lambda$ and $m_{k \ge 3} = 0$.
\item \emph{Open face} $= \cH_{-k}\text{-mod}$, the category of
  Fock modules, semisimple.
\item \emph{Spectral $R$-matrix}: $R(z) = e^{k\hbar/z}$, the
  abelian braiding.  The Yang--Baxter equation is satisfied
  trivially \textup{(}$R$ is scalar\textup{)}.
\item \emph{Bulk-to-boundary}: $\Abulk = \Zder(\cH_k) = \cH_k$
  \textup{(}abelian algebras are their own derived centers\textup{)}.
\item \emph{Genus face}: $\Theta_{\cH_k} = k \cdot \eta \otimes
  \Lambda$, recovering Vol~I\@.
\item \emph{PVA}: $\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$, the rank-$1$ PVA
  bracket.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
(i)--(ii): Propositions~\ref{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk}
and~\ref{prop:rosetta-operations}.
(iii): The spectral braiding of two Fock modules $\mathcal{F}_{\mu_1}$,
$\mathcal{F}_{\mu_2}$ at spectral separation~$z$ is
$R_{\mu_1,\mu_2}(z) = e^{k\mu_1\mu_2\hbar/z}$.  For the fundamental
module ($\mu = 1$): $R(z) = e^{k\hbar/z}$.
(iv): $\cH_k$ is commutative ($J_{(0)}J = 0$), so
$\HH^0(\cH_k) = \cH_k$ and the derived center is the algebra
itself.
(v): Restriction of $\alpha_{\cH_k}$ to the closed genus-expanded
sector gives $\alpha_{\mathrm{cl}} + \hbar\,\alpha_{\mathrm{loop}}
= m_2 + \hbar\,k\cdot\eta\otimes\Lambda = \Theta_{\cH_k}$.
(vi): $m_2(J,J;\lambda) = k\lambda$ descends to the PVA bracket
by the descent theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:cohomology_PVA}).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The doubling construction for Heisenberg}

The general half-space BV theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:general-half-space-bv}) is proved by the doubling
construction (Theorem~\ref{thm:doubling-rwi}): embed the half-space
into the doubled whole-space and apply Stokes.  For the Heisenberg
algebra, this construction is completely explicit.

The free propagator on $\C\times\R$ is
$K(z,t;\,z',t')=\Theta(t-t')/(2\pi(z-z'))$.  The reflected
propagator on the half-space $\C\times\R_{\ge 0}$ is
\[
K^{\mathrm{refl}}(z,t;\,z',t')
=\frac{\Theta(t-t')}{2\pi(z-z')}
+\frac{\Theta(t+t')}{2\pi(z-\bar z')}.
\]
The reflection $\sigma(z,t)=(\bar z,-t)$ maps
$\C\times\R\to\C\times\R$, and $K^{\mathrm{refl}}=K+K\circ\sigma$ is
the standard propagator on the doubled space restricted to the
$\sigma$-invariant sector.

The method-of-images embedding $\iota\colon\Conf_n(\C\times\R_{\ge 0})
\hookrightarrow\Conf_{2n}(\C\times\R)^{\sigma}$ sends each point
$(z_i,t_i)$ to the pair $\{(z_i,t_i),(\bar z_i,-t_i)\}$.  The
propagator form $\omega_{ij}^{\mathrm{refl}}=\iota^*(\omega_{ij}+
\omega_{i,\sigma(j)})$ extends to a logarithmic form on the
Fulton--MacPherson compactification of $\Conf_{2n}(\C\times\R)$, with
log poles along the collision divisor $z_i=z_j$ (standard) and the
reflection divisor $z_i=\bar z_j$ (from the image term).  Stokes'
theorem on the compactified doubled space gives the reflected weight
identity: the Heisenberg case is trivial (all higher operations vanish,
$m_k=0$ for $k\ge 3$), confirming
Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk} by a different route.

\section{The shadow tower atlas}%
\label{sec:shadow-tower-atlas}%
\index{shadow tower!atlas|textbf}%
\index{Lagrangian self-intersection!atlas}

The Heisenberg Rosetta Stone establishes the principle:
the bar complex of a chiral algebra is the structure sheaf of
a Lagrangian self-intersection, and the shadow tower
$\{\operatorname{Sh}_r\}_{r\ge 2}$ measures the excess
intersection multiplicity at each codimension.  For
$\cH_k$, the intersection is clean ($m_{k\ge 3}=0$) and the
tower terminates at arity~$2$: the single scalar~$k$ is the
only datum.  What happens for algebras with singular
self-intersections?

This section answers by explicit computation for all
standard families.  The results are summarised in the
\emph{shadow tower atlas}: a complete table of shadow
coefficients, depths, and termination arities for seven
families covering all four depth classes.

\subsection{Excess intersection depth}%
\label{subsec:excess-intersection-depth}%
\index{shadow depth!excess intersection}

\begin{definition}[Shadow depth as excess intersection
codimension]%
\label{def:shadow-depth-intersection}%
\index{shadow depth!definition}%
The \emph{shadow depth} of a chiral algebra~$\cA$
is
\[
  d(\cA)
  \;:=\;
  \max\{r \ge 2 : \operatorname{Sh}_r(\cA) \ne 0\}
  \;\in\;
  \{2, 3, 4, \ldots\} \cup \{\infty\}\,.
\]
In the Chriss--Ginzburg picture, $d(\cA)$ is the codimension
of the \emph{excess intersection locus}: the deepest stratum
of the Lagrangian self-intersection
$\mathfrak{S}_b = L\times_X L$ at which the Borel--Moore
fundamental class carries nontrivial convolution data.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{Four classes.}
\begin{center}
\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{cllll}

arity~$32$.

$V_k(\mathfrak{sl}_3) \to \cW_3$ sends a class~$\mathbf{L}$

(depth~$\infty$).  The depth increase is caused by the
composite field $\Lambda = {:}TT{:} - \tfrac{3}{10}

but is generated by the reduction.  DS reduction introduces
new self-intersection strata.

\begin{remark}[Chriss--Ginzburg reading of the atlas]%
\label{rem:cg-atlas}%
\index{Chriss--Ginzburg!shadow tower atlas}%
\index{Lagrangian self-intersection!depth classification}%
The depth classification
(Definition~\ref{def:shadow-depth-intersection}) has a
Chriss--Ginzburg interpretation: the shadow depth measures
the complexity of the Lagrangian self-intersection
$\mathfrak{S}_b = L\times_X L$.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \emph{Class~$\mathbf{G}$} ($d=2$): $\mathfrak{S}_b$ is
  a clean correspondence (smooth intersection, no excess).
  The Borel--Moore fundamental class is concentrated in the
  top stratum.  Archetype: $\cH_k$.
\item \emph{Class~$\mathbf{L}$} ($d=3$): the Lie bracket
  creates a transverse intersection defect at codimension~$1$,
  but the Jacobi identity kills all higher defects.  The
  excess locus has dimension~$1$.  Archetype: $V_k(\fg)$.
\item \emph{Class~$\mathbf{C}$} ($d=4$): a contact term
  from composite-field exchange creates an isolated
  codimension-$2$ defect; rank-$1$ abelian rigidity
  prevents further growth.  Archetype: $\beta\gamma$.
\item \emph{Class~$\mathbf{M}$} ($d=\infty$): composite
  fields generate an infinite tower of excess intersection
  strata.  The shadow tower does not terminate.  The Kac
  determinant factors accumulate in the denominators:
  $(5c+22)^{\lfloor(r-2)/2\rfloor}$ grows without bound.
  Archetype: Virasoro.
\end{itemize}
The $\cW_4$ two-channel phenomenon
(Computation~\ref{comp:w4-shadow-tower}) shows that the
class~$\mathbf{M}$ tower can have \emph{multiple independent
excess-intersection directions}: the $\Lambda$~channel and the
$W_4$~channel probe different strata of~$\mathfrak{S}_b$, with
different pole loci.  The Lee--Yang decoupling
($5c+22 = 0$ affects $\Lambda$ but not $W_4$) means the
self-intersection has at least two irreducible components
at codimension~$2$.
\end{remark}

\end{remark}

\section{The genus-1 endomorphism atlas}%
\label{sec:genus1-endomorphism-atlas}%
\index{endomorphism algebra!genus 1|textbf}%
\index{Lagrangian self-intersection!genus 1}%
\index{genus 1!endomorphism atlas}

\providecommand{\Mvac}{\cM_{\mathrm{vac}}}
\providecommand{\Mbar}{\overline{\mathcal{M}}}
\providecommand{\HH}{\operatorname{HH}}
\providecommand{\End}{\operatorname{End}}

The shadow tower atlas
(Computation~\ref{comp:shadow-tower-atlas}) classifies the
standard families by the excess intersection codimension of
the genus-$0$ Lagrangian self-intersection
$\mathfrak{S}_b = L \times_X L$: clean for class~$\mathbf{G}$,
transverse for class~$\mathbf{L}$, contact for
class~$\mathbf{C}$, infinite excess for class~$\mathbf{M}$.
All of this is genus-$0$ data, visible already on~$\C$.

At genus~$1$, the Lagrangian deforms
(Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}), the $r$-matrix
becomes elliptic
(Theorem~\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}), and the
five theorems of Volume~I acquire curvature.  Two
independent invariants control the deformation: the modular
characteristic $\kappa(\cA)$ (the scalar curvature, from the
highest-pole OPE coefficient) and the Coisson bracket
$c_0 = \{a \,{}_0\, b\}$ (the Lie-bracket residue, from the
simple-pole coefficient).  The characteristic controls the
closed colour; the Coisson bracket controls the spectral
braiding.  This section computes the genus-$1$
endomorphism algebra for every atlas family.

\subsection{The genus-1 atlas theorem}%
\label{subsec:genus1-atlas-theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Genus-$1$ endomorphism atlas;
\ClaimStatusProvedHere]%
\label{thm:genus1-endomorphism-atlas}%
\index{genus-1 endomorphism atlas|textbf}%
Let\/ $\cA$ be a chiral algebra from the shadow tower atlas
\textup{(}Computation~\textup{\ref{comp:shadow-tower-atlas}}\textup{)}
that is a logarithmic\/ $\SCchtop$-algebra.
The curved bar complex~$\barB^{(1)}(\cA)$
\textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}}\textup{)}
is determined by three genus-$1$ invariants.
In the Lagrangian picture
\textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:chiral-as-endomorphism}(iv)}\textup{)},
$\barB^{(1)}(\cA)$ is the genus-$1$ endomorphism algebra
$\cO\!\bigl(\cL_\cA^{(1)} \times_{\Mvac}
\cL_\cA^{(1)}\bigr)$:
the structure sheaf of the genus-$1$ Lagrangian
self-intersection.  The three invariants are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \textbf{Curvature.}
  $\dfib^{\,2} = \kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_1$, with period
  correction
  $F_1(\cA) = \kappa(\cA)/24$
  \textup{(}Corollary~\textup{\ref{cor:genus1-curved-swiss-cheese}}\textup{)}.

\item \textbf{Spectral type.}
  The genus-$1$ classical $r$-matrix
  $r^{E_\tau}(z) = c_0 \cdot \zeta(z|\tau) + c_1 \cdot \wp(z|\tau)
  + \cdots$
  \textup{(}Theorem~\textup{\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}},
  equation~\eqref{eq:elliptic-r-matrix}\textup{)}
  is doubly periodic if\/ $c_0 = 0$ \textup{(}Cartan type\textup{)}
  and quasi-periodic with $B$-cycle monodromy
  $2\eta_\tau \cdot c_0$ if\/ $c_0 \ne 0$
  \textup{(}Yangian type\textup{)}.

\item \textbf{Five-theorem projection.}
  Each of Volume~I's five theorems specialises at genus~$1$
  as a deformation of the genus-$0$ data by the single
  parameter $\kappa(\cA) \cdot \omega_1$
  \textup{(}following
  Proposition~\textup{\ref{prop:five-theorems-geometry}}\textup{)}:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\Alph*$_1$)},leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item \emph{Bar-cobar:} curved dg coalgebra
    $(\barB^{(1)}(\cA), \dfib, \Delta)$ over $\Mbar_1$;
  \item \emph{Inversion:} curved quasi-isomorphism
    $\Omegach(\barB^{(1)}(\cA)) \xrightarrow{\sim} \cA$
    in the coderived category;
  \item \emph{Complementarity:}
    $\kappa(\cA) + \kappa(\cA^!) = K(\cA)$ on the Koszul locus,
    where $K$ is a level-independent constant ($K = 0$ for the
    affine lineage; $K \neq 0$ for DS reductions);
    when $K = 0$, $\barB^{(1)}(\cA) \otimes \barB^{(1)}(\cA^!)$
    is uncurved;
  \item \emph{Leading coefficient:}
    $F_1 = \kappa(\cA)/24$ is the genus-$1$
    Faber--Pandharipande integral;
  \item \emph{Hochschild:}
    $\mathrm{CH}^\bullet(\cA)^{(1)}$ acquires a correction
    from the Hodge bundle
    $\mathbb{E} \to \Mbar_1$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

Atlas families in classes~$\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{L}$,
$\mathbf{C}$ are chirally Koszul (bar cohomology concentrated,
$m_k = 0$ for $k \ge 3$ on cohomology); class~$\mathbf{M}$
(Virasoro, $\cW_N$) is \emph{not} chirally Koszul---the
fourth-order pole causes purity failure and
$m_k \ne 0$ for all $k \ge 3$---but still possesses
well-defined Koszul duals (e.g.\
$\mathrm{Vir}_c^! = \mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}$).  The five-theorem
projection applies to the Koszul classes; for
class~$\mathbf{M}$, the bar-cobar adjunction holds but the
big Koszul dual carries higher $\Ainf$ operations.
Within the standard atlas families, the three
invariants $(\kappa, c_0, d)$ organise the atlas into three
genus-$1$ regimes, distinguished by their self-intersection
geometry and Coisson bracket:
\begin{center}
\small
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
\toprule
\textbf{Regime} & $c_0$ & \textbf{Intersection}
  & $R_1 \stackrel{?}{=} R_0$ & \textbf{Families} \\
\midrule
Decoupled & $= 0$ & clean
  & yes & $\cH_k,\; V_\Lambda$ \\
Propagator-entangled & $\ne 0$ & clean
  & no & $V_k(\fg),\; \beta\gamma$ \\
Doubly entangled & $\ne 0$ & excess
  & no & $\mathrm{Vir}_c,\; \cW_N$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Part~(i) is
Corollary~\ref{cor:genus1-curved-swiss-cheese}.
Part~(ii) is
Theorem~\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}, items
(\ref{item:dichotomy-decoupling}) and
(\ref{item:dichotomy-entanglement}).
Part~(iii) specialises
Proposition~\ref{prop:five-theorems-geometry} to $g = 1$
using Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}: the
Lagrangian $\cL_\cA$ deforms over $\Mbar_1$ with curvature
$\kappa \cdot \omega_1$, and each theorem is a facet of this
deformation.  Specifically:
(A$_1$) is
Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}(i)--(ii);
(B$_1$) is Theorem~B of Volume~I applied to the
curved coalgebra via the Positselski framework
(Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}(ii) and
Volume~I, Theorem~B(b));
(C$_1$) is Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}(iii);
(D$_1$) is the $g = 1$ specialisation of Volume~I,
Theorem~D\textsubscript{scal};
(H$_1$) is the genus-$1$ chiral Hochschild
correction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:hochschild-bridge-genus0}
extended over $\Mbar_1$ via the corrected differential
$\Dg{1}$).

The three-regime classification follows from
Theorem~\ref{thm:pole-structure-dichotomy}.
Classes~$\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{L}$, $\mathbf{C}$ are
chirally Koszul: the bar cohomology is concentrated,
the bar complex is cogenerated in degree~$1$, and
$m_{k \ge 3} = 0$ on cohomology.  For these,
Proposition~\ref{prop:curvature-braiding-decoupling}
applies: the bar-complex
channel~(\ref{item:bar-channel}) is trivial, the curvature
$\kappa \cdot \omega_1$ is primitive, and the $\Eone$-coproduct
is genus-independent.
Class~$\mathbf{M}$ (Virasoro, $\cW_N$) is \emph{not}
chirally Koszul: the fourth-order pole causes purity
failure, the PBW spectral sequence does not degenerate, and
bar cohomology is non-concentrated.  The bar complex has
higher-degree cogenerators (from wheel diagrams),
$m_{k \ge 3} \ne 0$ for all $k \ge 3$, and the
hypothesis of
Proposition~\ref{prop:curvature-braiding-decoupling}
(bar-degree-$1$ cogeneration) fails.  These algebras
still possess well-defined Koszul duals, but the big
dual carries higher $\Ainf$ operations that are absent
on the Koszul locus.  For these, the
bar-complex channel~(\ref{item:bar-channel}) can
contribute genus corrections.
The three regimes:
decoupled $\Leftrightarrow$ $c_0 = 0$ and $\Ainf$-formal
(conditions (ii)--(v) of
Theorem~\ref{thm:pole-structure-dichotomy});
propagator-entangled $\Leftrightarrow$ $c_0 \ne 0$ and
$\Ainf$-formal (bar-complex channel trivial by
Proposition~\ref{prop:curvature-braiding-decoupling},
propagator channel non-trivial by
Theorem~\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}(\ref{item:dichotomy-entanglement}));
doubly entangled $\Leftrightarrow$ $c_0 \ne 0$ and
$\Ainf$-non-formal (both channels can contribute, by
Theorem~\ref{thm:pole-structure-dichotomy}(vi)).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Heisenberg: all five theorems at genus~$1$}%

\begin{computation}[Heisenberg genus-$1$ endomorphism algebra;

\noindent
\emph{(A$_1$) Curved bar-cobar.}
$\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k) = T^c(s^{-1}\overline{\cH}_k)$
with $\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:rosetta-curved}).
The fiberwise differential on $E_\tau$ uses the
elliptic propagator $dG_\tau(z)$; the coproduct
$\Delta$ is unchanged.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{(B$_1$) Curved inversion.}
The cobar $\Omegach(\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k))$ inverts the
curved bar complex in the coderived category of
Positselski: the augmentation
$\Omegach(\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k)) \to \cH_k$ is a
quasi-isomorphism after restricting to the coderived
category.  This uses $m_{k \ge 3} = 0$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-heisenberg-mk}): the
Koszulness of $\cH_k$ ensures that the bar-cobar
unit is an equivalence fiberwise.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{(C$_1$) Complementarity.}
$\kappa(\cH_k) + \kappa(\cH_k^!) = k + (-k) = 0$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-complementarity}).
The tensor product
$\barB^{(1)}(\cH_k) \otimes \barB^{(1)}(\cH_{-k})$
has flat differential: the curvatures cancel, and the
genus-$1$ complementary Lagrangians remain
complementary.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{(D$_1$) Period correction.}
$F_1(\cH_k) = k/24$.  This is the first
Faber--Pandharipande coefficient
(Corollary~\ref{cor:genus1-curved-swiss-cheese}):
$\dfib^{\,2} = k \cdot \omega_1$ is compensated by
$\delta_{\mathrm{per}}$ satisfying
$\Dg{1}^{\,2} = 0$.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{(H$_1$) Hochschild at genus~$1$.}
The chiral Hochschild cochains
$\mathrm{CH}^\bullet(\cH_k, \cH_k)$
at genus~$1$ acquire a correction from the Eisenstein
series $E_2(\tau)$: the propagator
$G_\tau(z) = \zeta(z|\tau) + \frac{\pi^2}{3}E_2(\tau) z$
(equation~\eqref{eq:rosetta-elliptic-propagator})
introduces quasi-modularity into the Hochschild
differential.  The correction is proportional to $k$:
the Hochschild class $c_k \in H^2(\barB(\cH_k))$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:rosetta-bar-hochschild})
deforms by $k \cdot E_2(\tau)$.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{Spectral $r$-matrix.}
The $\lambda$-bracket $\{J_\lambda J\} = k\lambda$
has $c_0 = 0$ (no simple pole) and $c_1 = k$ (the
level).  By
Theorem~\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}(\ref{item:dichotomy-decoupling}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:heisenberg-genus1-rmatrix}
r^{E_\tau}_{\cH_k}(z)
\;=\;
k \cdot \wp(z|\tau),
\end{equation}
which is doubly periodic on $E_\tau$.  The spectral
$R$-matrix is unchanged from genus~$0$:
$R_1(z) = R_0(z) = e^{k\hbar/z}$
(Corollary~\ref{cor:rosetta-heisenberg-projections}(iii)).
The curvature $k \cdot \omega_1$ and the braiding
$k \cdot \wp(z|\tau)$ are decoupled: the former comes
from the $B$-cycle monodromy of $\zeta(z|\tau)$
acting on the bar differential, the latter from the
doubly periodic $\wp$-function which has no monodromy.
The $B$-cycle has no effect on $r$ because $c_0 = 0$
filters it out.
\end{computation}


\subsection{Affine $V_k(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$:

\label{subsec:affine-genus1-endomorphism}

\begin{computation}[Affine $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ genus-$1$
endomorphism algebra; \ClaimStatusProvedHere]%
\label{comp:affine-sl2-genus1-endomorphism}%
\index{affine Kac--Moody!genus-1 endomorphism}%
\index{elliptic quantum group!from genus-1 Lagrangian}%
\index{Yangian!elliptic deformation}%
For $V_k(\mathfrak{sl}_2)$ with generators
$J^a$ ($a = 1,2,3$) and $\lambda$-bracket

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{Period correction.}
$F_1 = 3(k+2)/96 = (k+2)/32$.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{Genus-$1$ $r$-matrix.}

Theorem~\ref{thm:elliptic-spectral-dichotomy}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:affine-genus1-rmatrix}

\;=\;
\Omega \cdot \zeta(z|\tau)
\;+\;

\end{equation}

$r^{E_\tau}(z + \tau) - r^{E_\tau}(z) = 2\eta_\tau \cdot \Omega$
(equation~\eqref{eq:r-monodromy}).  This is the
\emph{elliptic classical $r$-matrix}: the genus-$0$
rational $r$-matrix $r_0(z) = \Omega/z + k\kappa/z^2$
deforms by replacing $1/z \leadsto \zeta(z|\tau)$ and
$1/z^2 \leadsto \wp(z|\tau)$.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{The DK hierarchy and the genus tower.}
The genus expansion of the $r$-matrix parallels
the Drinfeld--Kohno hierarchy of quantum groups:
\begin{center}

$g = 1$ & $r_1(z,\tau) = \Omega\cdot\zeta + \cdots$

  & (conjectural) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

the rational kernel $1/z$ is replaced by
$\zeta(z|\tau)$ via

elliptic $r$-matrix of Belavin--Drinfeld.
At genus~$\ge 2$, the replacement of kernel functions
by their higher-genus counterparts (via the
period matrix $(\Omega_{ij})$ of $\Sigma_g$) produces a
candidate higher-genus quantum group structure,
but the identification is not proved.

\smallskip
\noindent
\emph{Entanglement.}
The curvature $\kappa \cdot \omega_1$ extracts $c_1 = k\kappa$
(the highest pole, a scalar); the braiding monodromy
$2\eta_\tau \cdot \Omega$ extracts $c_0 = \Omega$
(the lowest pole, a tensor).  Both arise from the
quasi-periodicity of $\zeta(z|\tau)$---the $B$-cycle is
the common geometric source---but they probe different
OPE pole orders.  The entanglement is one-directional:
the curvature is visible in the closed-colour
differential ($\dfib^{\,2} \ne 0$), the braiding monodromy is
visible in the mixed-colour $r$-matrix ($r_1 \ne r_0$),
but the open-colour coproduct $\Delta$ remains
flat at genus~$1$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:curved-swiss-cheese}(i)).
\end{computation}

\begin{remark}[The genus-$1$ dichotomy refines the depth
classification]%
\label{rem:genus1-dichotomy}%
\index{genus-1 dichotomy}%
\index{Coisson bracket!genus-1 classification}%
The shadow tower atlas
(Computation~\ref{comp:shadow-tower-atlas}) classifies by
the genus-$0$ shadow depth $d \in \{2,3,4,\infty\}$.
The genus-$1$ Coisson invariant $c_0$ provides an
orthogonal classification: among the seven atlas families,
$c_0 = 0$ picks out exactly the abelian algebras
($\cH_k$ and $V_\Lambda$), while $c_0 \ne 0$ picks
out everything with a Lie bracket or symplectic pairing.
The combined invariant $(d, c_0)$ produces the
three-regime classification of
Theorem~\ref{thm:genus1-endomorphism-atlas}:
the genus-$0$ depth controls the bar-complex channel
(clean for $d < \infty$, excess for $d = \infty$),
and $c_0$ controls the propagator channel.
Classes~$\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{L}$, $\mathbf{C}$ are
chirally Koszul; class~$\mathbf{M}$ is not (the
fourth-order pole causes purity failure and
non-concentrated bar cohomology).  The three-regime
distinction is within the standard atlas families,
not within a uniformly Koszul world.  What separates
class~$\mathbf{M}$ from the Koszul classes is precisely
a failure of chiral Koszulness: the PBW spectral
sequence does not degenerate, the higher $m_k$ from
wheel diagrams are non-trivial on cohomology, and
the bar-complex channel is activated by the excess
self-intersection.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The first modular coefficient and
question~\textup{(iii)} of the modular programme]%
\label{rem:modular-programme-genus1}%
\index{modular programme!first coefficient}%
\index{modular holography programme!genus 1}%
The period correction $F_1 = \kappa(\cA)/24$ is the
first genuinely modular coefficient requested in
question~(iii) of the modular holography programme
(Section~\ref{subsec:modular-holography-programme}).
In the Lagrangian picture, $F_1$ is the leading term
of the genus-$1$ self-intersection invariant
\[
R^{(1)}_\cA(z)
\;=\;
\chi\!\bigl(
  \cL_\cA^{(1)} \times_{\Mvac} \cL_\cA^{(1)}
\bigr)
\]
(Section~\ref{subsec:modular-holography-programme},
question~(iii$'$)).  The three regimes of
Theorem~\ref{thm:genus1-endomorphism-atlas}
answer this question as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},nosep]
\item \emph{Decoupled} ($c_0 = 0$):
  $R^{(1)}(z) = R^{(0)}(z)$.  The genus-$1$
  Lagrangian self-intersection is trivially deformed;
  the Euler characteristic is unchanged.  The only
  genus-$1$ effect is the scalar period correction
  $F_1 = \kappa/24$.

\item \emph{Propagator-entangled} ($c_0 \ne 0$, clean
  intersection):
  $R^{(1)}(z,\tau) = r^{E_\tau}(z)$ is the elliptic
  $r$-matrix~\eqref{eq:elliptic-r-matrix}, computed
  explicitly for all affine algebras and the
  $\beta\gamma$ system.  The genus-$1$
  self-intersection carries additional monodromy
  $2\eta_\tau \cdot c_0$ from the $B$-cycle of
  $E_\tau$.  The bar-complex channel is trivial
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:curvature-braiding-decoupling}).

\item \emph{Doubly entangled} ($c_0 \ne 0$, excess
  intersection):
  $R^{(1)}(z,\tau)$ receives corrections from both
  channels.  The propagator channel contributes the
  elliptic deformation; the bar-complex channel
  contributes corrections from the higher Swiss-cheese
  operations $m_{k \ge 3}$ (the wheel diagrams that
  source the infinite shadow tower).  The algebra
  is not chirally Koszul---bar cohomology is
  non-concentrated and $m_k \ne 0$ for all
  $k \ge 3$---though it retains a well-defined
  Koszul dual ($\mathrm{Vir}_c^! = \mathrm{Vir}_{26-c}$)
  carrying higher $\Ainf$ operations.  Computing
  $R^{(1)}_{\mathrm{Vir}_c}(z,\tau)$ explicitly is
  the concrete test case for question~(iii) of the
  modular programme.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[The genus tower as $\hat{A}$-genus;
\ClaimStatusProvedElsewhere]%
\label{thm:ahat-lagrangian}%
\index{A-hat genus@$\hat{A}$-genus!generating function}%
The total scalar free energy of the endomorphism algebra
$\End(\cL_\cA)$ expanded over $\coprod_g \Mbar_g$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:total-free-energy}
\boxed{
F^{\mathrm{tot}}(\cA;\, \hbar)
\;=\;
\frac{\kappa(\cA)}{\hbar^2}
\;\cdot\;
\frac{\hbar/2}{\sin(\hbar/2)}\,,
}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ahat-series}
\frac{x/2}{\sin(x/2)}
\;=\;
1 + \frac{x^2}{24} + \frac{7\, x^4}{5760}
  + \frac{31\, x^6}{967680} + \cdots
\;=\;
\sum_{g \ge 0}
  \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}}\, x^{2g}
\end{equation}
is the generating function for the unsigned
Faber--Pandharipande numbers
\textup{(}Volume~I,
Theorem~D\textsubscript{scal}\textup{)},
related to the Hirzebruch $\hat{A}$-class by
$\hat{A}(ix) = (x/2)/\sin(x/2)$---the Wick rotation
that turns the alternating-sign $\hat{A}$-series into
the all-positive genus expansion.
The genus-$g$ contribution is
$F_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}} \cdot
\hbar^{2g-2}$, with $\lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}} > 0$
for all $g \ge 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[The platonic content]%
\label{rem:ahat-platonic}%
\index{platonic ideal!A-hat genus@$\hat{A}$-genus}%
Equation~\eqref{eq:total-free-energy} is the platonic
content of the genus tower.  The Lagrangian
$\cL_\cA \hookrightarrow \Mvac$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:holographic-reconstruction-conclusion})
is a point in the derived symplectic category.  Its formal
neighborhood over $\coprod_g \Mbar_g$ is the
$\hbar$-expansion of the endomorphism algebra.  The
generating function of this expansion is
$(x/2)/\sin(x/2) = \hat{A}(ix)$---the Wick-rotated
$\hat{A}$-class, the same generating function that,
after rotation back, governs the index theorem for
Dirac operators.  The single scalar $\kappa(\cA)$ (the
modular characteristic) plays the role of the curvature;
$\hbar$ plays the role of the genus parameter.

\emph{Everything} in the monograph that depends on genus
is a coefficient of $(\hbar/2)/\sin(\hbar/2)$.  The
genus-$0$ constant term produces the flat Swiss-cheese
algebra; the genus-$1$ correction ($+\hbar^2/24$) produces
the curved Swiss-cheese algebra with curvature
$\kappa \cdot \omega_1$; the genus-$2$ correction
($+7\hbar^4/5760$) produces the next-order deformation
over $\Mbar_2$; and so on.  The five theorems at each
genus are the five facets of the single coefficient
$F_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Shadow corrections and the departure from
$\hat{A}$]%
\label{rem:shadow-corrections-ahat}%
\index{shadow corrections!departure from $\hat{A}$}%
The free energy~\eqref{eq:total-free-energy} is the
\emph{scalar} part of the Lagrangian expansion: it
captures the period corrections $F_g$ that restore
$D_g^2 = 0$ at each genus.  For algebras with clean
self-intersection (classes~$\mathbf{G}$, $\mathbf{L}$,
$\mathbf{C}$), this is the complete answer: the
higher-arity shadow corrections vanish, and the
$\hat{A}$-genus exhausts the genus tower.

For algebras with excess self-intersection
(class~$\mathbf{M}$: Virasoro, $\cW_N$), the full
genus-$g$ endomorphism algebra carries shadow corrections
$\delta F_g^{\mathrm{shadow}}$ from the infinite tower
(Computation~\ref{comp:virasoro-shadow-tower}):
\[
  F_g^{\mathrm{corr}}(\cA)
  \;=\;
  \underbrace{\kappa \cdot \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}}}_{\text{%
    $\hat{A}$-genus (scalar)}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\delta F_g^{\mathrm{shadow}}(\cA)}_{\text{%
    excess intersection (arity $\ge 3$)}}.
\]
The shadow corrections are the \emph{dispersive}
corrections to the genus tower---the same corrections
that appear in the Dubrovin--Zhang hierarchy as
higher-order terms beyond the dispersionless limit.
The $\hat{A}$-genus is the dispersionless approximation;
the shadow tower provides the full dispersive expansion.

For class~$\mathbf{M}$, the denominators of the shadow
coefficients $S_r$
(Computation~\ref{comp:virasoro-shadow-tower}) involve the
Kac determinants $\det M_r$: the self-intersection
degenerates where the algebra degenerates.  These
are the poles of the Lagrangian deformation over the
moduli of curves---the locus where the endomorphism
algebra fails to be a flat deformation.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[From one page to the book]%
\label{rem:atlas-continuation}%
\index{genus tower!atlas continuation}%
The genus-$1$ atlas
(Theorem~\ref{thm:genus1-endomorphism-atlas}) classifies
the first-order deformation by the triple
$(\kappa, c_0, d)$.  At genus~$g \ge 2$, the period
correction $F_g = \kappa \cdot \lambda_g^{\mathrm{FP}}$
(equation~\eqref{eq:faber-pandharipande}) extends the
scalar atlas automatically.  The new genus-$g$ data
enters through the period matrix $\Omega_{ij}$ of
$\Sigma_g$, which replaces the single modular parameter
$\tau$ of $E_\tau$: the elliptic $r$-matrix of
Section~\ref{subsec:affine-genus1-endomorphism} becomes
a function on the Jacobian of $\Sigma_g$, and the
$B$-cycle monodromy $2\eta_\tau \cdot c_0$ becomes a
$g \times g$ matrix of quasi-periods.

The genus-$0$ atlas classifies the point;
the genus-$1$ atlas classifies the first-order
deformation; the $\hat{A}$-genus generates the
all-genera scalar expansion; and the shadow tower
provides the dispersive corrections.  The modular
programme
(Section~\ref{subsec:modular-holography-programme})
is the problem of computing the complete dispersive
expansion at all genera for all standard families.
\end{remark}
